# Sanity-checking the D91 go and no-go bar for an early outdoor fitness product

> Raw research input received 2026-04-16. Frozen provenance. Curated synthesis lives in `docs/research/d91-retention-gate-evidence.md`; durable conclusions flow into `docs/decisions.md` (D91, O5) and `docs/discovery/phase-0-wedge-validation.md`.

## The evidence base is thin, but the directional pattern is usable

The first hard truth is that almost nobody publishes exactly the metric you want: **"second session within 14 days" in concierge or alpha cohorts under 20 people**. Public writeups from early consumer health and fitness teams usually report broader feasibility or retention markers instead: two-week completion, average weekly use, or medium-term cohort retention after launch. That means the most defensible answer is a triangulation, not a single benchmark.

In adjacent small-cohort physical-activity and habit-formation studies, **researcher-supported two-week pilots often show very high repeat or completion rates**. A 2024 digital health coaching usability test had **10 older adults and reported that all participants accessed the app daily for two weeks**. A 2025 physical-activity usability study of the M-PAC app had **14 inactive adults use the app for two weeks**, with feedback reported from **13 participants**, implying near-complete two-week follow-through. A 2023 walking intervention called SNapp ran a **2-week pilot with 11 users** and weekly interviews, again reflecting a highly managed pilot context. An earlier Habit app pilot for weight loss with **16 participants** reported **two to three app uses per week on average** during the 8-week intervention and acceptability data from **15 of 16** participants. These are not pure "organic" benchmarks, but they do set a realistic upper band for what hand-recruited and supported early cohorts can do.

Broad market health and fitness apps are much weaker. OneSignal's 2024 mobile benchmarks put **Health & Fitness** at **28% day-1 retention, 18.13% day-7 retention, and 8.48% day-30 retention**, while Adjust's 2023 health-tracker report showed **23% day-1, 16% day-3, and 12% day-7 retention** for the category. Those numbers are obviously not comparable to a handpicked alpha cohort, but they are a useful reminder that consumer fitness products usually decay fast once the app-store funnel replaces handholding.

The practical implication is this: if you are testing with **5 handpicked testers**, asking whether **3 of 5 return for a second session in 14 days** is **not unusually strict** relative to small, guided pilots. If anything, it is **below** the two-week completion levels reported in many supported feasibility studies. But it is also **far above** what a broad-market launched app could expect. So your bar sits in a plausible place for a **manual alpha sanity-check**, not for a scaled consumer benchmark.

## The main product frameworks all privilege repeated behavior over intent

Across Y Combinator, Reforge, guidance associated with Lenny Rachitsky, and the PMF method popularized by Superhuman, the common idea is the same: **revealed behavior matters more than what users say they might do**. YC's own material emphasizes retention as a core product metric, and YC-associated PMF guidance repeatedly frames real fit as users loving the product enough to keep using it and recommend it, not merely convert once. Michael Seibel is quoted by Lenny Rachitsky saying that if you are not seeing "explosive usage," you do not have PMF, and Sam Altman's YC-era advice centered on whether any users love the product enough to tell others spontaneously.

The strongest directly relevant artifact here is the Superhuman PMF process. Rahul Vohra did **not** survey all signups. He surveyed users who had experienced the product's core value and, specifically, followed Sean Ellis' recommendation to focus on people who had used the product **at least twice in the last two weeks**. That is not the PMF threshold itself; the PMF threshold in that framework is still the famous **40% "very disappointed"** result. But it does imply something important for your metric design: **two uses in 14 days is a reasonable minimum eligibility condition for asking whether someone has experienced enough value to judge the product seriously**.

Reforge's framing is more behavioral than numeric. Brian Balfour argues that retention is an output, while the actionable inputs are activation, engagement, and resurrection. He suggests mapping the path from signup to the **setup moment**, **aha moment**, and **habit moment**, then measuring where users drop out. His key point is that a single successful use is not the goal; the real question is whether the user reaches a repeat pattern that resembles a habit. Reforge's own writing makes the same point more bluntly: onboarding is only the first phase, and the hard part is getting the user to **build a habit** around the product.

Lenny's consumer PMF framework lands in the same place. He treats the cleanest PMF signal as **cohort retention curves that flatten**, because that shows some users continue to choose the product over time. He is careful, though: a curve flattening above zero means **some** segment wants the product, but a durable business needs the curve to flatten materially higher. For your use case, that means **a second session is evidence of non-zero pull**, not proof of a stable activation loop.

So the frameworks broadly support your instinct that **behavioral retention beats stated intent**. But they do **not** support treating **one return visit** as a strong PMF claim. In framework terms, your current bar is good as a **floor for "did they feel enough value to come back once?"** It is weak as a claim that you have found a real habit loop.

## The public consumer fitness case studies are uneven, but two are clearly instructive

Among the named companies, public material is most useful for Future and Strava. Public documentation for Whoop, Freeletics, Tonal, Tempo, Ladder, and Centr is much thinner on **explicit alpha pass/fail retention gates**. What those companies do discuss publicly is niche focus, accountability, beta traction, or testing with community feedback, not formal early-cohort thresholds.

Future is the closest public analogue to a concierge-validation story. At launch in 2019, TechCrunch reported that the product's beta tests had **95% of users still active after three months** and **85% still training after six months**, alongside a **one-month money-back guarantee that had only been redeemed once**. That is radically stronger than your D91 bar, but it is exactly what you would expect from a high-touch concierge model where accountability is the core product. The lesson is not that you should adopt Future's threshold; it is that in a concierge fitness setting, founders themselves leaned heavily on **hard repeat behavior** and continued willingness to stay enrolled, not on intention or satisfaction alone.

Strava is instructive in a different way. Mark Gainey has been explicit that the company's early go-to-market strategy was to go "inch wide, mile deep" into **passionate road cyclists**, because they were obsessive about data and underserved by existing products. He says Strava did grassroots recruiting, remembers the **first five cyclists**, and that Strava's **first customer was still on the platform 11 years later**. That is not a published alpha gate, but it is a strong clue about how the founders evaluated signal: they wanted repeated, durable use from a narrow segment whose behavior clearly reflected real pull. In other words, Strava did not start by asking whether casual users liked the idea of social fitness; it started by asking whether the most motivated niche kept coming back.

Freeletics is a third, weaker but still useful data point. The company says the original training concept was **repeatedly tested and improved based on community feedback** before and as the product took shape, and that its earliest appeal came from removing common exercise excuses like time, money, and equipment. Again, no formal threshold is published. But the evaluation logic is familiar: early teams looked for whether users kept incorporating the workouts into real life, not merely whether they found them interesting.

WHOOP's public origin story shows a different pattern: its first 100 users included elite athletes like LeBron James and Michael Phelps, and the company expanded through sports distribution partnerships. That tells you a lot about niche choice and credibility, but very little about a published early retention gate. For your specific question, the public record is simply sparse.

## Novelty effect is the biggest reason not to overread a second session

The literature on novelty effect cuts directly against any overconfidence in a "two sessions in 14 days" rule. In a mixed-methods study of Fitbit usage, Grace Shin and colleagues found that tracker use showed **two stages**: a **novelty period** and a longer-term use period, with the novelty period lasting **about three months**. They also cite prior work showing that in a 6-week Fitbit study, **half of participants abandoned the tracker after two weeks**. Their qualitative results are especially relevant: early use is often driven by **curiosity about the data** and **curiosity about the technology**, which are not the same thing as durable product value.

The digital health attrition literature says much the same thing more generally. A 2022 JMIR analysis notes that one common attrition pattern is users who **stay initially out of curiosity**, reflecting the novelty effect, and then drop out once the novelty wears off, leaving behind only a stable core. Another JMIR qualitative study argues that what matters is not sheer frequency of use but **effective engagement**: enough engagement to drive the desired behavior change, which may differ by product and user.

The older-adult tracker literature sharpens the distinction further. A 2019 JMIR focus-group study found that **former users' tracker use was fueled by curiosity in quantifying daily physical activity rather than the desire to increase physical activity**, and concluded that **initial positive response does not guarantee maintenance**. That is almost a direct warning against using a single return session as proof of value. It tells you that a second session may still be nothing more than "I want to see what this thing does."

For your product, the implication is clear. A second session inside 14 days is **necessary** evidence that the first session was not actively bad. But it is only **weakly sufficient** evidence that users found recurring value. The most defensible way to separate novelty from value in a 14-day window is to look for one of three stronger patterns: the second session happens **without human prompting**, it happens **late enough to cross out of the immediate curiosity spike** instead of the next-day impulse, or there is evidence of a **third use** or a concrete scheduling behavior that anchors the product into a routine. That last sentence is an inference from the novelty and habit-formation literature, but it is a strong one.

## The D91 bar is reasonable as a kill floor, but slightly too lax as a go bar

My bottom-line judgment is this: **your current D91 rule is appropriately calibrated as an early no-go screen, but slightly too lax as a go signal**. The specific kill rule — **fewer than 3 of 5 start a second session within 14 days** — is defensible. It asks for a simple majority return in a tiny, handpicked cohort, which is consistent with the idea that early fitness validation should be based on behavior, not claimed enthusiasm. It is also not obviously stricter than adjacent small-cohort pilot work, where supported two-week engagement is often materially higher.

Where the rule becomes weak is on the "go" side. Reaching **3 of 5 users returning once** does **not** show that an activation loop is real rather than noisy. The frameworks you named all point toward repeated, stabilizing behavior, and the novelty literature says one early repeat can still be explained by curiosity. So if D91 is treated as "good enough to keep building and keep testing," it is fine. If D91 is treated as "we have evidence of durable value," it is too lax.

The outdoor, sand-based, self-coached context cuts both ways. It probably **lowers raw repeat rates** versus a purely digital, always-available app because weather, location access, equipment, and self-directed setup friction all matter. That argues against an excessively strict threshold. But self-coached outdoor products also have **less built-in accountability** than a concierge human-coach product like Future, which means a single return is easier to misread: some users come back once out of curiosity, but without structure they disappear fast. That argues for not giving too much credit to two sessions alone. On balance, those factors cancel each other out enough that your majority-return rule looks sensible as a floor.

The cleanest way to say it is this: **"3 of 5 get to a second session in 14 days" is the right order of magnitude for determining whether there is any real pull at all. "5+ testers complete 2+ sessions in 14 days" is not strong enough, by itself, to call the product validated.** The metric is good for screening out dead-on-arrival ideas. It is not good enough to overcome the novelty-effect objection on its own.

## The most defensible interpretation of D91

If you want the current rule to remain intact, the most defensible way to talk about it is: **D91 is a sanity-check for initial behavioral pull, not a PMF threshold**. That wording squares with the public PMF frameworks, with Superhuman's "two uses in two weeks" filter, with Strava's niche-first repeated-use logic, and with the novelty literature. It also matches what your threshold can genuinely prove: that a majority of a tiny handpicked cohort found enough immediate value to come back once.

If you want a sharper answer to the literal question "is our current bar too strict, appropriately calibrated, or too lax?", the most honest answer is: **slightly too lax overall, but not by much**. It is **not too strict** for this stage. It is **appropriately calibrated as a kill signal**. It becomes **too lax only if passing it is treated as strong evidence of value rather than permission to keep testing**. The novelty-effect literature is the decisive reason for that conclusion.

Public evidence is incomplete on one point: among the specific companies you listed, only Future offers anything close to an explicit early retention benchmark in public sources, while Strava and Freeletics mostly give qualitative evidence about narrow-segment pull and repeated testing, and WHOOP, Tonal, Tempo, Ladder, and Centr do not appear to publish clear early alpha retention gates. That gap does not break the conclusion, but it does mean your best defense is a **framework-plus-analog evidence argument**, not a claim that there is a standard industry number everyone uses.
