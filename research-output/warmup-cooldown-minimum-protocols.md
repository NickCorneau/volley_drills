# Minimum Warm-Up and Cool-Down Protocols for Short Beach Volleyball Sessions

Raw research drop (2026-04-16). Frozen provenance for the curated note at
`docs/research/warmup-cooldown-minimum-protocols.md`.

## Recommended default

For beach-volleyball skill sessions lasting only 10–25 minutes, the most defensible product stance is this: make the **3-minute warm-up the default**, offer a **5-minute “best short-session” option** for users who can spare the time, and keep a **2-minute version only as a compliance-preserving fallback**. The literature does support warm-up for readiness and, when repeated consistently over time, for injury reduction. But it does **not** support claiming that a 2–5 minute block is equivalent to the validated 10–15 minute neuromuscular warm-ups used in volleyball and team sports. Validated programmes that reduce injuries are typically repeated at least twice weekly and are materially longer than the minimum your app is considering.

That means the right claim is narrower and more honest: a short mandatory block can still be evidence-based if it concentrates on the highest-yield pieces for this sport and surface—**brief whole-body ramp**, **ankle proprioception**, **shoulder and trunk activation**, and **sand-specific movement rehearsal**—while avoiding time sinks with weak evidence, especially prolonged static stretching and elaborate cool-downs. For short sessions, warm-up and cool-down should not be treated as symmetric; the evidence base for warm-up is much stronger.

## What the literature supports

**Volleyball-specific warm-up packages work, but they are longer than your product budget.** The best volleyball-specific programme in the literature is **VolleyVeilig**, developed as a 15-minute warm-up with a 2–3 minute cardiovascular ramp, 2–3 minutes of core work, 4–5 minutes aimed at knee injury prevention, 4–5 minutes aimed at shoulder injury prevention, and ankle work embedded throughout. In adult recreational volleyball, it produced a 21% lower acute injury incidence rate than control in one randomized trial; in youth volleyball, it was associated with reduced acute and upper-extremity injury rates plus substantially lower injury burden and severity. That is strong support for the *components* that matter, but it is not support for saying a 2-minute app block will reproduce the same effect size.

**Ankle proprioception is the single strongest individually validated component you can borrow.** In the classic volleyball balance-board trial, the intervention reduced ankle sprains overall and was especially effective for players with prior ankle sprain history. Broader meta-analyses show proprioceptive or balance training reduces ankle sprain risk in sport—especially recurrent sprains—and ankle bracing/taping also cuts recurrence risk substantially. In plain English: for ankle prevention, balance is evidence-based; isolated ankle mobility is not where the strongest data live.

**Shoulder-specific prep matters, but the evidence is smaller and more targeted.** A 2023 prospective volleyball study found that a shoulder-focused warm-up routine was useful for reducing injuries, with lower overall injury incidence in the intervention group. A volleyball trial using the football-derived **11+ Shoulder** also improved shoulder proprioception and dynamic stability. Separate prospective work identifies prior shoulder injury and scapular dyskinesis as meaningful risk factors. So a short shoulder block is justified, but the strongest direct evidence usually uses more structured exercises—often with bands or specific scapular/rotator-cuff work—than a bare-bones app can guarantee on court.

**Core and trunk work are worth keeping, but the evidence is more about burden and mechanism than headline injury-rate reduction.** In men’s volleyball, adding trunk and balance exercises did **not** reduce injury incidence, but it did reduce overuse-injury severity and overall injury burden. That fits the sport’s mechanics: shoulder pain is associated with trunk and lower-limb pain in overhead athletes, and low-back pain is a common overload problem in volleyball and beach volleyball. So a compact anti-extension or anti-rotation trunk drill is defensible, especially for lower-back and kinetic-chain reasons, but it should not be oversold as a directly proven rate-reduction tool on its own.

**Nordics and Copenhagen adductions are real evidence, but wrong fit here.** Nordic hamstring exercise programmes reduce hamstring injuries by about half across sports, and the Copenhagen adduction family has evidence for adductor strength and some groin-risk reduction in football. But those exercises are off-target for your main beach-volleyball mechanisms, are not beach-specific, and are poor fits inside a mandatory 2–5 minute warm-up because they are high-eccentric, compliance-sensitive, and potentially soreness-inducing. They belong in separate strength/prehab modules, not in the minimum block.

## How sand changes the job

Beach volleyball is not just indoor volleyball on a softer floor. Injury rates are lower in beach than indoor volleyball overall, and ankle sprains are less common on sand, but beach athletes still show a meaningful load of **shoulder** and **low-back** problems. In NCAA data, the shoulder was the most common injury site in beach volleyball, followed by the low back. In the long-running professional beach-volleyball injury data, low back, knee, and shoulder were the main overuse problems. So the short warm-up should **not** import indoor priorities wholesale; it should give relatively more attention to shoulder, trunk, and movement quality on sand than a generic indoor warm-up would.

The sand surface changes warm-up design in two important ways. First, sand has a **higher energy cost** and lower-impact profile than firmer surfaces, and sprinting/jumping on sand produces lower power and jump height with altered mechanics. Second, elite beach guidelines emphasize getting a feel for **wind and current conditions**, and explicitly prefer warm-up activities with a ball when practical. The implication is simple: on short sessions, do **fewer maximal jumps**, not more; use **low-volume rehearsal** instead of a long potentiation block; and, whenever possible, let the final moments of warm-up include a sand/wind-specific pattern, ideally with the ball.

There is also a hot-weather reality. Beach volleyball already has a formal heat-stress monitoring tradition, and sweat loss during tournaments is real. For a 10–20 minute skill session, extra warm-up time in hot sun is often a worse trade than a shorter warm-up plus hydration/cooling awareness. In very hot sand, safety interventions such as hydration, gradual first contacts, and foot protection matter more than adding more “mobility.”

## Warm-up specifications

The logic below is deliberate. The literature on team-sport performance generally favors **short active warm-ups of about 10–15 minutes** with rising intensity, and muscle temperature seems to rise fastest in the first 3–5 minutes. Dynamic stretching helps lower-limb explosive performance, with the best effects usually reported from **longer** total dynamic warm-up doses than your app can afford. Prolonged static stretching before explosive work is the wrong trade. So the minimum protocols below are **compressed best-available syntheses**, not “fully validated” equivalents.

**Beach Prep Two**
Use this only when session length is so tight that anything longer threatens main-block compliance.

1. **Build heat — 30 seconds.** March or easy jog in the sand with large arm swings.
   **Cue:** “Get lightly warm, not tired. Quiet shoulders, quick feet.” This is the minimum whole-body ramp and replaces static stretching as the first priority.

2. **Low shuffle and retreat — 30 seconds.** Two to three shuffles each way, then one quick retreat step and a controlled stop.
   **Cue:** “Stay low. Own the stop. Let the sand move under you.” This is a beach-specific rehearsal choice rather than a generic gym movement.

3. **Single-leg reach balance — 20 seconds left, 20 seconds right.** Stand on one leg with a soft knee; reach the free leg or opposite hand slightly forward and out, then recover.
   **Cue:** “Tripod foot. Knee soft. Don’t let the arch collapse.” This is the highest-yield ankle-prevention ingredient you can fit into a tiny block.

4. **Plank shoulder taps or mini-band external rotations — 20 seconds.**
   **Cue:** “Ribs down. Hips still. Shoulder blade moves, torso doesn’t.” With no equipment, shoulder taps are the better trunk-plus-shoulder compromise; if a mini-band is available, external rotations are more shoulder-specific.

**Thirty-second coaching prompt:** “Build heat for thirty. Shuffle and retreat for thirty. Balance left, balance right. Finish with shoulder taps—ribs locked, no sway.”

**Verdict:** defensible as a **minimum viable gate**, not as a fully protective warm-up. It covers the pieces with the strongest signal per second, but it is below the dose normally associated with meaningful whole-body readiness or programme-level injury reduction.

**Beach Prep Three**
This should be the standard app default.

1. **Build heat — 45 seconds.** Sand march to easy jog with arm circles and arm sweeps.
   **Cue:** “Breathe easy. Get warm enough to move fast.” Three minutes is the earliest point where a whole-body warm-up starts to look physiologically credible rather than symbolic.

2. **Alternating lunge with reach — 30 seconds.** Step into a shallow forward lunge, reach overhead, and rotate slightly toward the front leg.
   **Cue:** “Front heel heavy, back glute on, rotate through upper back.” This is included for hip/ankle/thoracic mobility and trunk organization; the evidence is supportive but less direct than the ankle-balance literature.

3. **Single-leg reach balance — 15 seconds left, 15 seconds right.**
   **Cue:** “Soft knee, quiet pelvis, stable foot.” This remains the ankle block because it is the most evidence-dense element available.

4. **Plank shoulder taps or scap push-up plus — 30 seconds.**
   **Cue:** “Long neck, ribs down, shoulder blades glide.” This is the fastest way to get both shoulder and trunk on-line without equipment.

5. **Sand-specific rehearsal — 45 seconds.** Preferred: self-toss, move, forearm pass/catch to yourself into the wind. No ball: shadow approach footwork and retreats with **zero or one** submax jump.
   **Cue:** “Read the wind. Smooth feet. No max efforts yet.” This is beach-specific coaching consensus and better use of scarce time than generic calisthenics.

**Forty-five-second coaching prompt:** “Forty-five seconds to build heat. Then lunge and reach. Balance left, balance right. Switch on the shoulder and trunk. Finish by reading the sand and the wind—move smoothly, don’t jump max.”

**Verdict:** the best overall trade for short sessions. It is short enough to protect practice time, but long enough to cover all key buckets without feeling fake.

**Beach Prep Five**
This is the best short-session warm-up if the user can spare it.

1. **Build heat — 60 seconds.** March, jog, and side-step continuously, adding controlled arm swings overhead.
   **Cue:** “Get lightly sweaty. Keep the effort under control.” This is the most important extra minute you gain versus the 2-minute option.

2. **Dynamic mobility — 60 seconds.** Alternate shallow lunge with overhead reach and squat-to-stand patterns.
   **Cue:** “Open the ankle, hip, and upper back without holding stretches.” Dynamic work fits the performance literature better than static holding in jumping sports.

3. **Ankle control and elastic wake-up — 60 seconds.** Twenty seconds left single-leg reach, twenty seconds right, then twenty seconds of low pogo hops or “ankle pops.”
   **Cue:** “Stick the balance. Then quick, tiny contacts—springy, not high.” The balance portion is strongly evidence-based; the pogo portion is more readiness-oriented and should stay low-volume.

4. **Shoulder and trunk block — 60 seconds.** Thirty seconds scap push-up plus or band external rotation, then fifteen seconds side plank each side.
   **Cue:** “Shoulders glide, ribs stay down, hips stay stacked.” This is the strongest short-session way to respect the beach shoulder/low-back burden without dragging in a full prehab circuit.

5. **Beach rehearsal — 60 seconds.** Preferred: self-toss pass/catch, soft set, and one to three controlled approach patterns, with at most two submax jumps at roughly 70–85%.
   **Cue:** “Feel the court, feel the wind, save your max jumps for the session.” Sand suppresses jump output and costs more energy, so rehearsal should be specific but low-fatigue.

**Sixty-second coaching prompt:** “Build heat. Open hips, ankles, and upper back. Own one-leg balance, then wake up the lower leg with quick small hops. Switch on shoulders and trunk. Finish by feeling the sand and the wind with smooth, submax volleyball movement.”

**Verdict:** this is the most defensible “short but serious” version. It is still shorter than the evidence-based programme doses that dominate the prevention literature, but it is the version with the best chance of actually changing readiness without cannibalizing the main block.

**What not to include in these minimum versions.** Do not make prolonged static stretches mandatory before play. Do not spend a minute on generic toe-touching, quad holds, or hamstring holds. Do not insert Nordic hamstrings, Copenhagen adductions, or multiple maximal approach jumps into a 2–5 minute warm-up. Those are either evidence-light for the target problem, too fatiguing, or both.

## Cool-down specification

The evidence after 2015 is pretty blunt: **active cool-downs clear blood lactate faster, but they usually do not meaningfully improve soreness, muscle-damage markers, neuromuscular recovery, range of motion, or psychological recovery compared with passive recovery**, and post-exercise stretching is not a strong recovery tool either. Some active-recovery studies find performance benefits with **6–10 minute** protocols, but that is outside your budget and far longer than makes sense after a 15-minute skill session. So the app should stop calling the post-session block “injury prevention” and instead frame it as **transition, downshift, and comfort**.

**Downshift**
Use this as a **2–3 minute** post-session default.

1. **Easy walk — 60 to 90 seconds.** Walk the sand slowly.
   **Cue:** “Long exhale. Let heart rate come down.” This is the only part that is clearly worth keeping for everyone.

2. **Calf and foot unload — 30 seconds.** Gentle calf pumps or a light standing calf stretch, alternating sides rather than holding hard.
   **Cue:** “Ease out of the sand, don’t crank the stretch.” This is a comfort choice, not a proven recovery intervention.

3. **Hip flexor and trunk reach — 30 seconds.** Split stance, reach overhead, gentle exhale, switch sides.
   **Cue:** “Open the front of the hip, keep the rib cage quiet.” Again: comfort and movement reset, not hard evidence for recovery.

4. **Shoulder reset — 30 seconds.** Cross-body shoulder reach or wall/lat reach if available.
   **Cue:** “Gentle tension only.” This is optional and should be skipped if it irritates the shoulder.

5. **Hydration and symptom check — 10 to 15 seconds.**
   **Cue:** “If pain is rising, note it now. Hot session? Rehydrate before you leave.” In beach settings, this is more useful than pretending the cool-down itself is protective.

If you want the most honest product label, call this block **“Downshift”** or **“Transition”**, not “Recovery” and definitely not “Injury Prevention.” That is better aligned with the evidence.

## Product language and review flags

For naming, stay descriptive and generic. Avoid branded labels linked to programmes such as those from entity["organization","FIFA","world football body"]’s 11+ family unless you have a licensing reason to use them. Public-facing fitness copy already tends to use non-proprietary verbs such as **mobility**, **activation**, **stability**, and **springy movements**. Good app-safe module names are **Build Heat**, **Prime Ankles**, **Shoulder and Trunk Prep**, **Rehearse the Sand**, and **Downshift**.

The best in-app safety line is short and blunt. Something like **“Sharp pain, dizziness, numbness, tingling, or pain that climbs rep to rep: stop and switch to easy walking.”** That matches common exercise-disclaimer language better than a long legal paragraph, and it is much more likely to be read. If you need a second line, use **“Skip any movement that hurts today.”**

The biggest places where expert review is warranted before shipping are these:

- **Shoulder exercise choice.** If the product can assume a mini-band or wall, a more shoulder-specific option is possible; if not, the no-equipment version is a practical compromise with weaker direct specificity.
- **Progressions for prior ankle sprain.** The evidence is strongest in previously sprained athletes, and these users may deserve an app branch that adds bracing guidance or a slightly longer balance drill.
- **Pain branches for current shoulder or low-back irritability.** A physiotherapist should review substitutions for players who cannot tolerate plank, side plank, or overhead reaching.
- **Youth, novice, and return-to-play variants.** The minimum versions above assume a generally healthy athlete; they should not be the only version in the product if novice or previously injured users are a major audience.
- **Hot-sand and heat policy.** Review whether the app should explicitly mention hydration, session truncation, or sand socks under very hot conditions.

Finally, these are the components I would explicitly flag inside the product as **evidence-light or tradition-heavy**:

- mandatory pre-session **static stretching**;
- any claim that **isolated ankle mobility** has the same preventive value as balance/proprioception;
- **pogo hops** as an Achilles/calf readiness tool, which is a practical coaching inference rather than a directly validated beach-volleyball prevention element;
- post-session **stretching for recovery**;
- any statement implying the **2-minute warm-up** has the same injury-reduction evidence as VolleyVeilig or other 10–15 minute neuromuscular programmes.

The cleanest ship-ready position is therefore: **default to Beach Prep Three, allow Beach Prep Five, keep Beach Prep Two as fallback, and relabel cool-down as Downshift.** That is the shortest package I can defend from the volleyball, warm-up-dose, and sand-specific literature without pretending the evidence says more than it does.
