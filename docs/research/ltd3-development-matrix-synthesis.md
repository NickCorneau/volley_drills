## The LTD 3.0 stage framework in one diagram

```
                       LTD Stage (age range)              Practice : Compete
Active Start           birth–6                            play-led
FUNdamentals           6–8 (girls) / 6–9 (boys)           80% play / 20% instruction
Learn to Train         8→AGS (girls) / 9→AGS (boys)       70 / 30
Train to Train         during adolescent growth spurt     60 / 40
Learn to Compete       ~16–18 (f) / 17–19 (m)             40 / 60
Train to Compete       provincial / junior-national       40 / 60
Learn to Win /
Train to Win           Olympic / Paralympic / Worlds      individualized
Active for Life        any age; after foundation stages   recreational / masters / fit-for-life
```

The VDM sits inside this framework and fills in Person / Athlete / Player
content per stage. For the Matrix layer, use
`docs/research/vdm-development-matrix-takeaways.md`.

## Target-user mapping (Active for Life → Train to Train content)

The single most actionable claim in this stack for Volleycraft's target
user — a self-coached amateur adult beach player — is from `LTD 3.0 p. 53`:

> Masters athletes may have previously been in high-performance programs
> or may have taken up the sport later in life. Those with prior high
> performance experience have likely learned most of what they need to
> know about the sport, while those who take up the sport in their 20s,
> 30s, 40s or even later, **will benefit from developing the skills and
> the capacities outlined in this document for the Train to Train stage.**

The *stage label* for our user is **Active for Life** (specifically the
Fit-for-Life or Competitive-for-Life sub-phase, depending on whether they
enter organized rec competition). The *content scope* Volleyball Canada
recommends they work through is **Train to Train**. That pairing —
Active-for-Life stage, Train-to-Train content — is an authoritative anchor
for Volleycraft's target-fit framing that was previously informal.

Life phases inside Active for Life (`VDM 2025 p. 21`):

- **30s.** "Effort begins to switch from growth to maintenance… new
  parents frequently suffer a decline in physical activity… it is critical
  for adults to prioritize their time to include rest, respite from child
  rearing, activity and competition outlets, and social connections."
- **40s and 50s.** "Muscle strength and endurance begin to insidiously
  erode, while recovery from minor injuries takes longer and major
  injuries may permanently limit movement. Engaging in strength training
  1–2 times/week can help maintain strength and body composition. This is
  also an important time to consider transitioning from high to low
  impact sports."
- **50s and 60s.** "Physical activity can be a means of optimizing
  well-being and quality of life." Emphasis on weekly activity protecting
  weekend intensity and on exercise classes as social infrastructure.
- **70s–90s.** Resistance training shown to lift mood; sitting time
  correlated with worse health outcomes.

See [S1](#seeds) for the onboarding-signal seed this motivates.

### V1. "Do not train if you are fatigued or sleep deprived" (Active for Life)

`VDM 2025 p. 24` prints a stage-by-stage sleep table. The Active-for-Life
row — explicitly addressed to "any age participant", which is our target
— gives four key-point recommendations:

- Target 7–9 hrs/night plus a 30-minute nap between 2–4 pm; keep sleep
  debt to a minimum.
- Maintain meal routines and always eat breakfast.
- Learn to nap.
- **Do not train if you are fatigued or sleep deprived.**

Mapping to Volleycraft: this validates the basic shape of
`SafetyCheckScreen` (training-recency and pain-override gates) and the
existing decision (`D83`) that favors a short readiness check over a long
wellness form. The "do not train if fatigued or sleep deprived" phrasing
is an authoritative variant of the copy the safety screen already exposes,
and it is *not* a medical claim — it is Volleyball Canada's
training-recommendation language for the Active-for-Life participant. The
narrower VDM note scoped sleep/sustenance out of its mining pass; the
Active-for-Life row specifically deserves to be cited because it is
addressed to our target user by name.

### V2. Practice-to-compete ratios support the solo-first bias

The LTD one-pagers give practice : compete ratios running from 70 / 30
(Learn to Train) to 60 / 40 (Train to Train) to 40 / 60 (Learn to Compete
onward). LTD 3.0 also treats organized competition as over-emphasized for
early-stage development (`LTD 3.0 p. 39`):

> While it is a critical piece of the sport experience and an important
> component of every periodized plan, it is often given too much emphasis,
> particularly during the first few years of an athlete's development.
> This can have a detrimental effect on individual skill development, on
> building an athletic base of strength and power and, most importantly,
> on providing a fun and enjoyable sport environment.

Mapping to Volleycraft: our current posture — **solo-first,
practice-first, with optional scored-play variants** — aligns with the
Train-to-Train 60 / 40 ratio Volleyball Canada recommends for adult
beginners. This strengthens the solo-first wedge (`D6`) against expansion
pressure toward "we should also ship tournament tools" during Phase 0.

## Person Pillar Guidebook as a coaching-copy template

The Person Pillar Guidebook is the newest and most distinctive piece of
this source set. Unlike the VDM (which is a reference document), the
Guidebook is a coaching-behaviour manual. For each dimension of the Person
pillar it prints four artifacts:

1. **Desired state.** One sentence describing the target.
2. **Indicators of desired state / undesired state.** Observable
   behaviours — what the coach should be watching for.
3. **Reinforcing comments.** Three templated things to say when you see
   desired-state behaviour. Example structure (`Person_Pillar_Guidebook`
   pp. 4–6):
   - "I've noticed that [insert indicator]. This is great! Keep it up."
   - "I noticed you've been making efforts to [insert indicator]. Great
      work today!"
   - "I've seen you [insert indicator] and it is having a great impact on
      your/the team's success/improvement. Way to go!"
4. **Reflection questions.** Three templated things to *ask* when you see
   undesired-state behaviour. Example structure:
   - "I've noticed that [insert indicator]. Are you noticing something
      similar? (Just because you see something doesn't make it true or
      real.)"
   - "What are some of the reasons why this might be happening?"
   - "What can we do to improve in this area?"

The Guidebook is explicit that praise needs no behavioural cause —
getting curious allows the athlete to reflect and actively participate in
their growth, and reinforcing what the athlete does well encourages them
to continue. That tone — observe, reinforce, question, *do not judge* — is
exactly the tone Volleycraft's rules-first / explainable-adaptation
posture already aspires to (`D6`, `D11`, related decisions).

Dimensions covered (each with its own page):

- **Common Needs** — Physiological, Safety, Belongingness, Esteem.
- **Connection** — to the present moment, to self, to others.
- **Care** — for self, for others, for groups.
- **Courage** — to take risks and fail, to commit and persevere.

The existing `vdm-development-matrix-takeaways.md` explicitly ruled the
Person pillar out of scope because it "coaching soft skills [are] not
actionable for a 1-2 adult amateur self-coached courtside tool." That is
true for the **VDM's short Person pillar section** (1 page of indicators).
It is **not** true for the Guidebook — which is 30 pages of templated
copy, structured exactly the way a well-designed post-session review
screen, journal prompt, or weekly receipt wants its copy structured. See
[S2](#seeds) for the concrete seed.

## Seeds

Items below are candidate product hooks suggested by the sources. None of
them are in M001 scope. They are captured so that, if / when the relevant
phase activates, the evidence base is already sitting next to existing
canon.

### S1. Life-phase onboarding signal

`VDM 2025 p. 21` carves the Active-for-Life population into 30s, 40s–50s,
50s–60s, 70s–90s buckets with distinct physiological posture and lifestyle
constraints. A single one-tap onboarding question ("Roughly which life
phase are you in?") could condition:

- recovery framing ("your 40s" → "recovery takes longer than it used to"
  as training-language, not medical-language)
- strength-work framing (40s–50s → "1–2 times/week is plenty to keep what
  you have")
- high-impact warnings at the *framing* level, never at the *restriction*
  level (see
  `docs/research/regulatory-boundary-pain-gated-training-apps.md` for the
  avoid-phrase list)

Candidate open question for `docs/decisions.md`: *"Should life phase
become a first-class onboarding signal alongside skill level and training
recency?"* Signals to watch: whether the `D91` field-test cohort
spontaneously cites age / life-phase concerns in the 72-hour non-returner
probe.

### S2. Person-pillar copy layer from the Guidebook

The Person Pillar Guidebook's four-artifact template (desired state /
indicators / reinforcing comments / reflection questions) is a
ready-to-port scaffold for several Volleycraft surfaces:

- **Review screen post-session prompt bank.** Instead of reducing to a
  "how did it go" slider, cycle through Care-for-self, Connection-to-
  present-moment, and Courage-to-commit themed reflection questions. The
  Guidebook's three-question template is a drop-in structure.
- **Journal prompt library.** For sessions that complete with good load
  and unremarkable technique, a Care / Connection / Courage themed prompt
  gives the session meaning without fabricating a "lesson" the app didn't
  earn.
- **"Explain the plan" surface.** The rules-first / explainable-adaptation
  posture already calls for reasons. Frame the reason as a **reinforcing
  comment** when progress holds ("I've seen you complete three sessions
  in a row with comfortable load — this session builds on that") and as a
  **reflection question** when the engine de-loads ("The load has drifted
  above what you've been holding. What are some of the reasons this might
  be happening?"). This matches the Guidebook's observe / reinforce /
  question tone exactly and never crosses into diagnosis.

The Guidebook is also useful for *what not to do*. Its explicit note that
"just because you see something doesn't make it true or real" is a guard
against over-confident app-driven interpretations — which matches the
regulatory posture (`D86`) that treats self-report signals as low-fidelity.

### S3. Off-court coverage (Sustenance / Sleep / Structural Tolerance)

The VDM treats Sustenance, Sleep, and Structural Tolerance as first-class
peers to the physical 4 Ss. Volleycraft currently addresses sleep
(indirectly, via the training-recency and fatigue gates) and structural
tolerance (directly, via the sRPE-load engine and the deload rule) but has
no sustenance surface. A future "off-court week" coverage that is **not**
a nutrition prescription but a set of reminders keyed to stage-appropriate
Volleyball Canada language (pre-, during-, and post-session nutrition
framing from `VDM 2025 p. 22`) is a low-effort adjacency. It is out of
scope for M001 and arguably out of scope for Phase 1.

Note: this is explicitly off-court coverage, not a new in-session gate.
`docs/research/vdm-development-matrix-takeaways.md` item 4 already owns
the Structural Tolerance *copy-layer* seed for the in-session sRPE engine;
this seed is the weekly / off-session surface instead.
