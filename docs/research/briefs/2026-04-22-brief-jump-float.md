# Research brief: Jump-float serve biomechanics and amateur introduction protocols

> This brief is intended to be sent to an external research vendor. The body below is self-contained — no prior project context is required. Copy or forward the content below the "--- BRIEF ---" marker as needed.

--- BRIEF ---

## Context

We are designing a local-first mobile training application for adult amateur beach volleyball players who train without a coach. Target users are adults with two to five years of recreational experience, training one to three times per week. The app will include a structured serving progression that introduces the jump-float serve as an intermediate-level skill step.

Jump-float serving involves a ball toss, a short approach, a jump, and a no-spin contact in mid-air. It is known to load the shoulder (particularly posterior structures) and is typically introduced under coach supervision in organized training. In a coach-less, self-administered context, a mobile app shipping a "jump-float introduction" drill carries real risk — both injury risk if volume or technique is wrong, and skill-acquisition risk if progression prerequisites are skipped.

We need evidence-based defaults for introducing this skill safely to amateur adults without supervision.

## Research question

For adult amateur beach volleyball players at the intermediate skill level who are learning the jump-float serve, what are the evidence-based best practices for safely introducing the skill and managing cumulative shoulder load? Specifically:

- What per-session volume caps are defensible?
- What prerequisite skills and attributes should be in place before introduction?
- What biomechanical cues matter most for reducing shoulder injury risk?
- What criteria define successful progression from stand-float to jump-float?

## Why this matters

A training app shipping a drill with the phrase "three sets of fifteen serves" has made a specific clinical recommendation by implication. If the actual evidence supports twelve reps per set, or if a specific warm-up is required, or if certain rotator-cuff prerequisites matter, the app's default should reflect that. We want the most conservative, defensible set of defaults a self-coached user can follow without supervision — plus a clear separation of "this has research backing" versus "this is practitioner wisdom."

## Key sub-questions to address

1. **Injury profile.** What is the documented injury profile for amateur beach volleyball servers, especially shoulder (supraspinatus, SLAP, subacromial, long head biceps, posterior impingement, GIRD)? Incidence rates where available. Any specific association with jump-float versus stand-float or jump-topspin?
2. **Volume thresholds.** What per-session serve-volume thresholds have been associated with elevated injury risk in amateur populations? How do they differ between stand-float, jump-float, and jump-topspin? Weekly volume thresholds if available.
3. **Prerequisite physical profile.** What shoulder strength, mobility, and movement-quality markers should be in place before a player attempts jump-float? Specifically address scapular control, overhead ROM, rotator cuff internal/external rotation strength ratios, and any field-testable screens a non-clinician could apply.
4. **Progression sequence.** What intermediate steps exist between "stand-float with consistent accuracy" and "full approach jump-float"? List the recommended sequence with rationale per step (e.g., stand-toss-and-jump, one-step approach with no contact, full approach with contact to target zones).
5. **Biomechanical cues.** What biomechanical cues do credible sources emphasize for beach-specific jump-float (toss height, toss placement, foot-plant position, body alignment, contact point, follow-through, wind adjustment)? Rank by injury-relevance where evidence supports ranking.
6. **Per-session caps.** What rep counts, set counts, and rest ratios do credible coaching sources (FIVB, USAV, physios) recommend for players introducing this skill? Call out when a recommendation has research backing versus practitioner-wisdom backing.
7. **Warning signs.** What acute or cumulative warning signs should a player stop on? (Posterior shoulder ache, loss of external rotation, loss of accuracy consistent with fatigue, pain with overhead reach hours or days later, etc.)
8. **Sand-specific effects.** How does sand (versus wood or rubber floor) change load at the foot-plant and takeoff phase? Does sand reduce peak jump height enough to reduce shoulder load indirectly, or does the unstable surface add other injury risks (ankle, knee)? What is known from comparative biomechanics studies?
9. **Time-to-competence.** What is the typical time-to-competence for an amateur adult learning jump-float (weeks or months of consistent practice at what frequency)? Any research on whether rushed introduction increases long-term injury risk?

## Deliverable

A 2,000–4,000 word memo containing:

- **Executive summary** (200–400 words) — top-line per-session volume cap recommendation, top prerequisite checks, and top three biomechanical cues, each with stated confidence level.
- **Injury profile section** — what is known about amateur beach-serve injuries with a focus on shoulder. Incidence rates with sample sizes where available.
- **Progression sequence section** — recommended intermediate steps between stand-float competence and full jump-float, with rationale per step.
- **Per-session volume and load section** — defensible rep caps, set structures, rest ratios, frequency limits, with citations and evidence-level ratings per recommendation.
- **Prerequisites section** — what "ready for jump-float" looks like, including any field-testable screens a non-clinician user could apply self-administered.
- **Warning-signs section** — acute (stop-now) and cumulative (reduce-volume or see-physio) red flags.
- **Sand-specific considerations** — what differs from indoor, with evidence.
- **Practitioner-wisdom section** — coaching cues and progressions practitioners emphasize, clearly separated from formal research.
- **Gaps section** — what the literature does not answer.
- **"Conservative defaults for a courtside app" section** — if we had to ship a single recommendation without coach or physio oversight, what is the most defensible rep cap, prerequisite check, and warning-sign panel? This section will be read as the minimum-viable ship spec.
- **Full citation list.**

## Priority sources

- **Tier 1 (peer-reviewed sports medicine):** Journal of Shoulder and Elbow Surgery, American Journal of Sports Medicine, Journal of Athletic Training, British Journal of Sports Medicine, International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance, Clinical Journal of Sport Medicine. Overhead-athlete shoulder-load literature generally, with volleyball-specific studies prioritized.
- **Tier 2 (governing bodies and physiotherapy protocols):** FIVB coaching and medical materials, USA Volleyball, AVCA coaching curricula, AMSSM and ACSM position statements on overhead-athlete management, published physiotherapy protocols for volleyball shoulder.
- **Tier 3 (strength and conditioning):** NSCA position papers, NASM materials on overhead-athlete conditioning, sport-specific strength and conditioning literature.
- **Tier 4 (practitioner):** Published methodology from established beach volleyball coaches (Karch Kiraly, Mike Dodd, April Ross, or equivalent) where they address serving progression or introduction protocols. Coaching blogs and video tutorials from credentialed practitioners for cues only, clearly flagged as practitioner wisdom.

## Constraints

- Target population: **adult amateur**, training 1–3 times per week, **without professional coaching or physiotherapy supervision**.
- Bias toward **conservative defaults** appropriate for a self-coached context where the user cannot be supervised.
- Recommendations that assume a supervised rehabilitation or training context should be explicitly flagged as "requires supervision" and separated from solo-defensible guidance.
- Any rep-count or volume threshold must be clearly labeled as either research-backed (with citation) or practitioner-wisdom-backed (with source).

## Things to flag explicitly

- Specific rep counts or volume thresholds that have research-backing versus practitioner-wisdom-only backing. We need to know which is which when authoring default session content.
- Progression steps with known failure modes (common injury patterns linked to specific progression shortcuts).
- Anything the researcher would red-flag as "do not ship without physio input" — i.e., guidance that carries enough injury risk that shipping it in a self-coached app is irresponsible regardless of caveats.
- Any evidence that jump-float is categorically inappropriate for certain amateur subpopulations (e.g., players with prior shoulder history, players outside a certain age band, players without baseline overhead mobility).

## Success criteria

The memo is successful if a product designer and a consulting physiotherapist can read it together and:

- Agree on a conservative default rep cap and rest ratio to ship.
- Agree on a minimum prerequisite check the app surfaces before introducing the drill.
- Agree on a warning-sign panel the app surfaces during or after the drill.
- Understand which of the above are research-backed versus practitioner-wisdom-backed.
- Identify the single biggest gap in the evidence that would require real-world monitoring post-launch.
