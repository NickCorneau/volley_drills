# Safely introducing the jump-float serve in a self-coached beach volleyball app

## Executive summary

**Top-line recommendation (moderate confidence).** For unsupervised adult amateur beach volleyball players (2–5 years recreational experience, training 1–3×/week), ship a **conservative per-session cap of 30–40 full-effort jump-float serves**, inside an overall ceiling of **~60–75 total overhead reps per session**, with **≥48 hours between high-volume serving sessions** and **never three consecutive serving days**. These numbers are practitioner-grade extrapolations — no volleyball study has established a validated per-session serve-count threshold. The closest direct data (Wolfe et al. 2019, NCAA Division I women) observed mean 37.7 total overhead swings per in-season practice and 47.1 pre-season, with the higher pre-season volume coinciding with a significantly higher injury rate (2.9% vs 1.1%, p=0.042). Reeser et al. 2010 (Sports Health) established that jump serves produce shoulder internal-rotation torques approaching those of a full spike (~40 N·m vs ~32 N·m for standing float), supporting treating the jump-float as a mid-load skill — lower than jump-topspin but meaningfully higher than standing float.

**Top three prerequisite gates (moderate confidence).** Before the app unlocks jump-float progression, the user should clear (1) a **pain-provocation screen** (no sharp or painful-arc pain on active full elevation or supine 90/90 internal/external rotation), (2) an **ER/IR strength ratio ≥0.75** on the dominant side via a bathroom-scale-and-doorjamb proxy (Intelangelo 2025 identified ratio <0.75 as the single most prevalent risk factor among 296 amateur overhead athletes), and (3) **active shoulder flexion ≥160°** with <10° side-to-side asymmetry, measured with a smartphone inclinometer (ICC 0.90–0.96 vs goniometer; Keller 2023; Shimizu 2022).

**Top three biomechanical cues (high confidence where flagged).** (a) **Contact in front of the hitting shoulder with a short, stiff, flat-hand strike and compact follow-through** — mechanically distinguishes jump-float from jump-topspin and keeps peak internal-rotation torque in the lower range (Reeser 2010; research-backed, high confidence). (b) **Maintain ~120–130° shoulder abduction at contact and avoid hyper-horizontal-adduction** ("hitting across and behind the body") — volleyball's ~130° abduction and 29° horizontal adduction at contact is a proposed driver of posterior-internal impingement and suprascapular-nerve traction (Reeser et al. 2013 BJSM; research-backed, moderate-high confidence). (c) **Deliberate knee flexion in cocking, "slow-faster-fastest" approach tempo, and a jump-straight-up (not into court) on sand** — kinetic-chain engagement lowers absolute shoulder internal-rotation torque (~56 Nm vs ~64 Nm) while preserving ball speed (Reeser 2010; moderate confidence, research plus practitioner).

**Single biggest evidence gap:** no peer-reviewed study has directly quantified jump-float shoulder kinetics (only stand-float and "jump serve" pooled), and no prospective study has established per-session or per-week serve-volume thresholds in amateur beach players. All numerical caps in this memo are conservative extrapolations; post-launch monitoring is essential.

## Injury profile for the amateur beach server

**Shoulder is the dominant site of overuse burden in beach volleyball.** Across the best available cohorts, weekly in-season shoulder-problem prevalence is ~19% and lifetime history among competitive players is 46–60%. In the NCAA Division I beach volleyball cohort of Juhan et al. 2021 (n=53 beach players, 170 beach injuries over 17 seasons), **shoulder was the single most common beach injury site (15.3% of all beach injuries)**, and mean time lost per shoulder injury was **52 days on beach vs 28 days indoor** (p=0.001). Aagaard 1997 (295 players) found beach injury rate 4.9/1000 volleyball-hours (vs 4.2 indoor), with shoulder as the dominant overuse site on sand. Bahr & Reeser's FIVB study of 188 pros found shoulder among the top three overuse sites. The closest proxy for amateurs — Reeser, Joy et al. 2010 PM&R, n=422 collegiate club players — found **~60% reported a history of shoulder problems**, with attackers and jump-servers significantly over-represented versus setters, defensive specialists, and float-servers. Wang & Cochrane 2001 found 46% lifetime shoulder-injury history in English Division 1 men; spiking caused 79% of first shoulder injuries; mean chronic-pain duration was 2.3 ± 1.3 months. De Toledo et al. 2025 (n=95, prospective, 8 months) reported shoulder pain incidence 52.6% and injury 38.9%.

**Pathology-specific findings.** Subacromial bursitis and rotator cuff (especially supraspinatus) tendinopathy dominate: in De Toledo's MRI subsample of injured players, subacromial bursitis appeared in 94%, SLAP in 73%, and AC arthritis in 62%. Skazalski 2021 linked supraspinatus tendon neovessels and bursal thickening to symptoms on ultrasound. SLAP lesions show very high asymptomatic MRI prevalence in elite volleyball (Lee et al. 2020, JISAKOS: **46% of asymptomatic US Olympic volleyballers had SLAP changes**). GIRD in volleyball is typically an anatomic adaptation (mean ~10°; Challoumas 2017 systematic review) — unlike baseball, where it is reliably pathologic — but pathologic GIRD (commonly defined as IR deficit ≥18–20° with total rotational motion deficit ≥5°) is associated with ~1.5× impingement risk (Schmalzl 2022) and in volleyball specifically with shoulder pain (Alqarni 2022: pain group GIRD 15.7° vs 9.1° no-pain, p=0.004).

**Infraspinatus atrophy and suprascapular neuropathy are the nearly-sport-specific signals.** Lajtai 2009 (84 pro beach, n=29 MRI) found **30% infraspinatus atrophy in the hitting shoulder**; Lajtai 2012 confirmed **34% atrophy with EMG/NCV evidence of suprascapular-nerve changes**. These are typically painless — Constant score differed by only ~6 points between atrophied and healthy — which is the key product-design implication: pain-based monitoring alone will miss this signal. Mazza 2020 reported lower contemporary prevalence (9% M / 12% F) in elite indoor, attributing the decline to the shift from float to jump-topspin serves and implicating the **arrested-follow-through of the float strike** (terminal-branch traction at the spinoglenoid notch) as the classic biomechanical driver (Ferretti 1987; Witvrouw 2000).

**Serve-type associations.** Reeser 2010 Sports Health (biomechanical, n=14 + n=5 jump) ranks shoulder kinetic load: **spike ≈ jump serve ≫ stand float > roll shot**. Peak shoulder internal-rotation torque was 31.9 ± 8.2 N·m for standing float, 40.3 ± 10.4 N·m for the jump serve, and 36.8 ± 9.1 N·m for cross-body spike. Reeser's companion epidemiologic paper (PM&R 2010) found **jump servers significantly more likely to report shoulder problems than float servers** — the most direct serve-type-to-injury linkage in the literature. Jump-float itself has never been kinetically measured as a distinct category; on biomechanical grounds (shorter contact, no topspin whip, lower linear velocities per Ikonomou 2013 and Huang & Hu 2007) its shoulder load is reasonably inferred to sit between stand-float and jump-topspin, probably at ~65–80% of spike torque. **This is the single most important evidence gap** underlying every volume recommendation that follows.

**Subpopulations where jump-float is categorically inappropriate or requires clinical clearance** (each warrants a hard gate in-app): prior shoulder surgery; symptomatic SLAP or rotator cuff tear; pathologic GIRD >25° or IR deficit with symptomatic shoulder; visible infraspinatus atrophy or unexplained ER weakness; prior glenohumeral dislocation or instability episode with Beighton ≥5/9; active neurologic symptoms (numbness/paresthesia); age ≥50 without overhead-throwing history (asymptomatic rotator cuff tear prevalence is 20–31% at 60–69 and 36–51% over 70, Tempelhof 1999; Minagawa 2013 — jumping directly into high-volume overhead serving unsupervised is not defensible). Women and female adolescents carry higher rates of non-traumatic overuse shoulder complaints (Frisch 2017) — not a contraindication, but an argument for tighter volume defaults.

## Progression sequence from stand-float to full jump-float

Synthesis of FIVB Coaches Manual Level II, Volleyball Canada development module, USA Volleyball coach interviews ("Five Keys to a Wicked Jump Float Serve," McCutcheon, Kiraly, Ross, Speraw, Hill), Better at Beach (Burik/Joyner, AVP), Art of Coaching Volleyball, Gold Medal Squared, and the biomechanics of MacKenzie 2012 and Reeser 2010.

**Stage 1 — Contact mastery (no toss, no jump).** The server strikes a self-held or partner-held ball with a firm, flat, stiff palm; audible "thud, not slap"; palm finishes pointing at target. *Competency to advance:* 5 consecutive spin-free contacts [practitioner wisdom]. *Rationale:* Hugh McCutcheon (USAV): "contact is where the rubber meets the road." Grooving a centered, spinless strike establishes the aerodynamic prerequisite for knuckleball flight (Reiser 2020 Frontiers quantified the drag-crisis float effect at ~12–18 m/s). *Failure mode if skipped:* wiping/finger-roll contact → spin → easy pass AND compensatory shoulder "fishing" movement under dynamic conditions.

**Stage 2 — Standing float with toss.** Staggered stance, 6-inch "lift" toss in front of the hitting shoulder, step-and-strike with bow-and-arrow loaded position. *Competency:* ≥80% in-bounds over 20 reps, minimal/no spin, ball crosses net low [practitioner wisdom; Kiraly "flean" — flat + clean]. *Rationale:* builds hip/shoulder rotation with ground support and grooves toss consistency (FIVB Level II manual). *Failure mode:* "early arm dominance" — players who skip this stage tend to toss too high later to "buy time" for their feet, producing late contact and eccentric-overload arm-fishing.

**Stage 3 — Stand-toss-and-jump (no approach / small hop).** Stationary toss, vertical hop, strike near apex. *Competency:* contact at/near apex, zero spin, ≥70% in-bounds over 15 reps. *Rationale:* isolates timing of release to moving body without approach complexity. *Failure mode if skipped:* servers going straight from standing to full approach habitually toss too high, producing late contact — the classic mechanical precursor to deceleration-phase rotator-cuff overload (Reeser 2010; research-backed rationale, practitioner-backed staging).

**Stage 4 — One-step approach.** Single step with non-dominant foot as the ball lifts, then jump-to-strike. *Competency:* toss synchronized with first step, ≤1 ft forward drift, ≥75% in-bounds. Mirrors Burik/Joyner's "step-close and hit a smooth standing shot" drill. *Failure mode:* rushing the first step, producing "fast-fast-fast" tempo rather than the "slow, faster, fastest" cue (Gold Medal Squared).

**Stage 5 — Full approach jump-float (2–3 step), low tactical demand.** L-R-L (right-handed) approach matching a basic spike-approach pattern, toss released as the penultimate foot touches the sand; contact at full arm extension at apex of a **vertical** (not forward-drifting) jump. *Competency:* error rate ≤20% in a 25-serve set. *Critical beach modifier* (April Ross): "jump more straight up and rely on arm-swing speed" — beach court is 8m vs 9m indoor, so forward drift creates foot-faults and sailed serves. *Rationale:* MacKenzie 2012 showed a jump-focused technique produces higher contact height, flatter trajectory, and higher initial serve speed than a contact-focused technique — full approach unlocks that production envelope (research-backed). *Failure mode if skipped:* shortcutting approach-sequence training produces arm-only effort; Reeser 2010 (PM&R) epidemiology directly links this profile to elevated shoulder-problem rates in jump-servers.

**Stage 6 — Tactical jump-float with target zones and wind adaptation.** Full approach with deliberate zone targeting; wind-side selection; short-vs-deep deception via follow-through modulation. *Competency:* ≥90% in-bounds (Speraw: "error rate lower than 10%"), 2-of-3 zone accuracy [practitioner wisdom; Jim Stone reports 93% in-bounds as USA U-18 benchmark]. *Rationale for the app:* this is the "weaponization" stage; injury-relevant only insofar as cumulative volume should be managed rather than avoided.

**Practitioner time-to-competence estimate for amateur adults training 2–3×/week (not research-validated):** Stages 1–2 in 2–4 weeks; Stage 3 in a further 2–4 weeks; Stage 4 in 2–4 weeks; Stage 5 (functional in rally) at 3–6 months cumulative; Stage 6 at 6–18 months. No peer-reviewed study has examined learning time-course or the relationship between rushed progression and injury in this population.

**Defensible app defaults for this population.** Per session: ≤30–40 full-effort jump-topspin (treat as spike-equivalent), ≤50–60 full-effort jump-float, ≤60–80 standing-float, combined overhead cap ≤60–75 reps for developing servers and ≤100 for experienced with clean technique. Within-set cap of 8–10 serves before a 6-minute rest. Inter-rep rest 20–30 s. Per week: aim for acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR) of 0.8–1.3 on a 7-day acute / 28-day chronic window, with the app flagging yellow at ≥1.1 and red at ≥1.3 for unsupervised users (tighter than the published 1.3/1.5 because of self-coached context); no more than a 10% week-over-week volume increase; ≥48 h between high-volume serving sessions; **never three consecutive full-effort serving days** (PitchSmart extrapolation, consensus). Annual: ≥4 continuous weeks complete rest from overhead serving; ≥3 months of reduced overhead load (PitchSmart consensus; extrapolated).

**Critical caveat for the product team.** Every numerical rep cap above is a practitioner extrapolation, not a research-validated threshold. No peer-reviewed study has established a session or weekly serve-count threshold for shoulder injury in volleyball, let alone in amateur beach players. The ACWR framework itself is disputed (Andrade 2020 systematic review) and its volleyball application rests on elite-cohort data (Timoteo 2021/2025). The conservatism bias is intentional: in an unsupervised self-coached context, the cost of an overestimate (missed training stimulus) is small relative to the cost of a shoulder injury requiring a median 6-week time loss (Verhagen 2004) — up to 52 days on the beach (Juhan 2021).

## Prerequisites and self-administered screens

The three-factor framework for overhead-athlete shoulder risk is well established (Cools 2015; 2022 Bern Consensus, JOSPT): **rotational ROM, rotator-cuff strength balance, and scapular control**. Almost none of the validated clinician versions of these tests were designed for self-administration, and Bahr 2016 (BJSM) warns that screening tests do not reliably predict individual injury — they should flag modifiable deficits, not "clear" athletes. With that framing, the following self-administered screens have sufficient validity and practicality to ship.

| # | Screen | Method (self-admin) | Threshold (fail) | Validity | Gating vs informational |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Pain provocation | Active full flexion + supine 90/90 ER and IR; any reproducible sharp or painful-arc pain >2/10 | Any positive | Clinical heuristic (Hawkins-Kennedy-derived) | **Gating + "do not ship without physio input"** |
| 2 | Active shoulder flexion | Smartphone strapped to distal humerus, raise arm overhead | <160° OR >10° side-to-side | Research-backed (ICC 0.90–0.96 vs goniometer; Keller 2023 OJSM, Shimizu 2022 JSES Int) | **Gating** |
| 3 | ER/IR strength ratio | Bathroom/luggage scale against doorjamb, elbow at side 90° flexed; max isometric ER then IR; 2 trials | Dominant ER/IR <0.75 | Research-backed threshold (Cools 2015, 2016; Intelangelo 2025 prospective 296 amateur overhead athletes: <0.75 was **the most prevalent risk factor**) | **Gating** (with built-in ER-strength corrective block before unlock) |
| 4 | Supine 90/90 IR (GIRD check) | Smartphone on forearm, cue scapula flat | IR deficit >18–20° or TRM deficit >5°; flag >25° as urgent | Research-backed threshold; self-admin validity **low** (no scapular stabilization) | Gating only if deficit **>25°** (borderline → clinician-recommended) |
| 5 | Scapular Dyskinesis Test | Tripod video from behind, light dumbbell, 5 flexion + 5 abduction | "Yes" — obvious winging/dysrhythmia | Expert inter-rater κ=0.85; self-rating untested | Informational; **gating + physio flag** if obvious winging |
| 6 | CKCUEST (closed-chain upper extremity stability) | Two tape lines 91 cm apart; push-up touches in 15 s, 3 trials | <15 touches | Research-backed reliability ICC 0.92–0.97; Pontillo 2014 <21 predicted shoulder injury in D-1 football | Gating at <15; informational 15–21 |
| 7 | Beighton + prior instability | 9-point self-score + history | ≥5/9 + prior dislocation/subluxation | Research-backed; self-admin valid | **Gating + "do not ship without physio input"** |
| 8 | Horizontal adduction, UQ-YBT, thoracic extension/rotation | Smartphone inclinometer, tape-measure reach | Side-to-side asymmetry; <45° T-rotation | Research-backed reliability; not predictive alone | Informational — prescribe correctives |

**What genuinely cannot be assessed without a clinician:** true passive GIRD (requires scapular stabilization by a clinician; self-measurement overestimates IR); scapular assistance/retraction tests (partner-dependent); rotator-cuff provocation tests beyond pain reproduction; isokinetic strength; and high-validity SDT rating.

**"Do not ship without physio input" prerequisites (require clinician review of app copy):** the GIRD threshold language (18° vs 20° vs 25° is genuinely contested in the literature); the age-50 cutoff; the scapular dyskinesis self-rating instructions; all "clinician-clearance-required" gates for prior surgery, symptomatic SLAP or rotator cuff tear, and prior instability.

## Warning signs

### Acute — STOP immediately and seek care (any single item)

Any one of these should auto-escalate to the highest-severity app panel and end the session. Supporting sources: AAOS OrthoInfo; Burkhart, Morgan & Kibler 2003 (Arthroscopy — "disabled throwing shoulder"); Wilk 2009 JOSPT; Bern Consensus 2022.

Sudden sharp pain during the serve motion, especially late-cocking external rotation or ball contact (possible acute SLAP or rotator cuff tear). Audible or palpable pop/click with loss of function (labral tear, biceps rupture, subluxation). "Dead arm" — sudden sharp pain in max ER with inability to serve with normal control (anterior subluxation + internal impingement triad). Numbness, tingling, or paresthesia radiating down the arm (brachial plexus traction, cervical radiculopathy, thoracic outlet). Visible deformity, sudden bruising, or arm-won't-work sensation. Acute large loss of active ROM after one session (cannot elevate above 90° or rotate externally). Pain severe enough to change mechanics mid-serve. Drop-arm phenomenon (arm gives way overhead).

### Cumulative — reduce volume or see a clinician

Posterior shoulder ache persisting >24–48 h post-session (chronic eccentric infraspinatus overload). Morning stiffness in the dominant shoulder lasting >24–48 h. **Progressive loss of internal rotation (GIRD ≥18° or TRM deficit ≥5°)** — research-backed threshold (Wilk 2011 AJSM). Within-session loss of velocity or accuracy attributable to fatigue — Lyman 2001 (extrapolated from Little League) identified fatigue as the strongest single correlate of shoulder injury in young overhead athletes. Delayed-onset overhead pain hours after session. **Visible infraspinatus fossa hollow or asymmetry** — this is the single most volleyball-specific and most-likely-to-be-missed signal; Lajtai 2009 documented 30% prevalence in pro beach volleyball with only ~6 Constant-score points of performance deficit, so pain-based monitoring alone will miss it. ER weakness relative to contralateral side on self-test. Scapular winging/dyskinesis that worsens with volume ("SICK scapula"; Burkhart/Morgan/Kibler 2003). Night pain in the dominant shoulder (classic rotator cuff pathology flag). Progressive ER-strength loss over weeks (Clarsen 2014 BJSM showed this prospectively predicted overuse shoulder injury in handball). Training-load spike >60% vs four-week rolling average.

### PROM-based escalation (research-backed thresholds)

**KJOC <90 (AMBER); <80 (RED "see physio within 2 weeks"); <70 (RED-URGENT).** The Kerlan-Jobe Orthopaedic Clinic Shoulder & Elbow score (Alberta 2010 AJSM) is the gold-standard overhead-athlete PROM; asymptomatic pros average ~93 (Kraeutler 2013), symptomatic ~71 (J-KJOC cutoff 87.6). **FAST >10/100 (AMBER); >20/100 (RED)** — Functional Arm Scale for Throwers, Bliven 2017 OJSM, cutoff 10 gave 91% sensitivity / 75% specificity for upper-extremity injury. **Pain NRS ≥4/10 during serving (AMBER); ≥6/10 or any mechanics-altering pain (RED-STOP).** Two or more AMBER flags concurrent, or any AMBER persisting >14 days despite volume reduction → RED.

**Caveats for the product team.** KJOC and FAST licensing/permissions must be confirmed before in-app reproduction. SPADI has ceiling effects in high-functioning athletes — do not use as a primary PROM. OASES in the original brief appears to refer to the KJOC itself (the "Overhead Athlete Shoulder and Elbow Score" naming is used for KJOC in some papers); no separate validated OASES instrument was identified and this should be confirmed with the consulting physio.

## Sand-specific considerations

**Jumping.** Sand consistently reduces vertical jump heights by ~9–14% versus rigid surfaces (Giatsis 2004, 2018, 2022; Bishop 2003; Tilp 2008). Squat-jump peak vertical GRF is ~8% lower on sand, peak power is reduced, ankle ROM and angular velocity are larger (posterior foot slippage), and hip extension range increases. Drop-jump power is lower but rate of force development is higher on sand (Giatsis 2022). Crucially for this memo: **upper-limb angular velocities during the spike jump do not differ significantly between sand and indoor** (Tilp 2008). This supports the inference that arm/shoulder biomechanics of serves on sand closely mirror indoor measurements, while lower-limb take-off is substantially modified. Foot-plant on sand is flatter and more parallel, to distribute load and prevent sinking; eccentric-concentric transition at the knee is slower because sand absorbs elastic energy.

**Landing.** No direct force-plate study exists of volleyball-specific landings on sand versus hard court, but mechanical reasoning and ecological evidence both suggest sand substantially reduces peak landing vGRF (softer surface → longer penetration → extended impulse). Lian et al. 2005 found patellar tendinopathy prevalence dramatically lower in elite beach than elite indoor players, consistent with reduced cumulative landing load. Sand training also produces significantly less muscle damage (CK, myoglobin) and less DOMS than equivalent intensity on harder surfaces (Impellizzeri 2008 BJSM; Miyama 2004; Brown 2017).

**Implications for the serving app.** The jump-float is particularly appropriate for sand because it requires only a submaximal jump — the sand-induced jump-height penalty (~10%) has minimal performance cost for this serve but a larger cost for jump-topspin. Shoulder cues transfer directly from indoor to beach (Tilp 2008 arm-velocity invariance). Beach-specific cues are primarily lower-body: expect deeper countermovement, flatter foot-plant, slower eccentric phase — these are natural adaptations, not faults. Ankle sprain rates are reported lower on beach than indoor (Bahr & Reeser 2003), consistent with reduced landing forces despite surface instability. The standard beach cue to "jump straight up" rather than drifting forward (April Ross via USAV) is driven by court dimensions (8m vs 9m) and foot-fault risk, not by shoulder load.

**Critical gap:** no peer-reviewed study has measured GRF, shoulder torque, or ball velocity during any serve (jump-float, stand-float, or jump-topspin) on sand versus hard court. All sand biomechanical data come from isolated SJ/CMJ/DJ tasks. Wind-adjustment mechanics are coaching tradition only — no biomechanical literature exists.

## Practitioner wisdom — clearly separated from research

The beach volleyball coaching community converges on a tight set of cues and progression heuristics that are widely taught but have not been independently validated biomechanically. These should be flagged in the app as practitioner wisdom rather than evidence-based prescription.

Karch Kiraly and Kim Hill (USAV) emphasize "flean" contact — flat plus clean, spin-free, low-arc trajectory. April Ross emphasizes jumping straight up rather than into court on sand and serving into a crosswind for maximum movement. Mark Burik and Brandon Joyner (Better at Beach, AVP pros) prescribe a "six-inch toss," heel-of-palm contact, and a three-drill progression (stationary contact → step-close → full approach). Gold Medal Squared (Carl McGown lineage) teaches "slow, faster, fastest" approach tempo. Jim Stone (USA U-18, 93% in-bounds competency bar) teaches a four-step approach with toss release on step 2 and contact at full arm extension. Angie Akers (Art of Coaching Volleyball) specifies wind-adjustment cues: "lasers" to shoulders in headwind, high floats with tailwind. Phil Dalhausser's reported Mikasa-vs-Wilson ball-type rationale for float-only serving at FIVB events is widely cited but anecdotal.

Formal coaching curricula that document aspects of these progressions include the FIVB Coaches Manual Level II and Volleyball Canada's Jump Float Serve development module; USA Volleyball's published coach interviews aggregate these cues but do not publish a formal rep-by-rep progression. AVCA and CEV curricula specific to this progression were not found in publicly indexed form. Kiraly's *Beach Volleyball* (1999, Human Kinetics) is the most authoritative single beach-specific text from a credentialed practitioner but was not directly accessible for page-level citation.

**Practitioner cues with plausible but unverified injury relevance:** (a) the arrested follow-through that produces float aerodynamics is the same mechanism historically implicated in suprascapular neuropathy (Ferretti/Reeser/Mazza) — volume management, not technique avoidance, is the injury-relevant lever; (b) high toss heights to "buy time" for footwork produce late contact and shoulder-fishing patterns; (c) "early arm dominance" (serving with arm only, no hip rotation or weight transfer) is mechanistically plausible as an overuse driver and should be addressed in-app via video-review prompts.

## Gaps in the literature

No peer-reviewed study has directly quantified jump-float serve shoulder kinetics. All biomechanical comparisons pool "jump serve" (typically jump-topspin). No study has compared stand-float, jump-float, and jump-topspin shoulder load within the same cohort. No force-plate study of any serve on sand vs hard court. No prospective injury-incidence study in adult amateur beach players (2–5 years experience, 1–3× per week). No validated per-session or per-week serve-count threshold for shoulder injury — the entire literature on volume is either descriptive (Wolfe 2019), rehabilitation-derived (Hurd 2009), or extrapolated from baseball pitching (PitchSmart, Fleisig 2011). The pathologic GIRD threshold in volleyball is unsettled (10–25° across papers); Alqarni 2022 proposes 10–18° with TRM >8° specifically for volleyball. ACWR "sweet spot" thresholds (0.8–1.3) are derived from professional team sports with daily monitoring and have not been validated for self-tracked amateurs. No biomechanical study of wind-adjustment technique. No peer-reviewed learning-curve data for adult amateurs learning jump-float. The Pieber 2014 vs Lajtai 2012 conflict on whether beach infraspinatus atrophy is truly neurogenic vs biomechanical/disuse remains unresolved. No validated OASES instrument distinct from KJOC was identified.

### Do-not-ship-without-physio-input items

These pieces of guidance carry enough injury risk that shipping in a self-coached app without explicit sports-physiotherapist review is not defensible. (a) Any PROM reproduction (KJOC, FAST, QuickDASH) — licensing plus threshold text. (b) GIRD threshold language (research range 10–25° — pick one and be transparent). (c) Age-≥50-without-overhead-history gate — conservative extrapolation from asymptomatic rotator cuff tear prevalence, not a volleyball-specific trial. (d) Infraspinatus-atrophy self-check copy — because the finding is typically painless, reassurance framing must be physio-drafted to avoid false reassurance. (e) "Continue to play?" decision logic for any detected cumulative red flag. (f) Suprascapular neuropathy education copy. (g) All clinical-clearance gate copy for prior surgery, instability, symptomatic SLAP or rotator cuff tear.

### Minimum-viable ship spec (balanced column summary)

For a single-number ship decision before physio review: cap jump-float at **40 per session**, combined overhead at **75 per session**, require **≥48 h between sessions**, forbid three consecutive serving days, enforce **ACWR ≤1.3 with ≤10% weekly progression**. Gate on **pain provocation + active flexion ≥160° + ER/IR ratio ≥0.75**. Show warning at **pain NRS ≥4, post-session ache >24 h, visible scapular asymmetry, numbness or night pain** — auto-escalate to "see a physio" at **pain ≥6, two AMBER flags concurrent, KJOC <80, or FAST >20**. Every numerical threshold should be presented to users as "evidence-informed estimate" rather than validated cutoff.

## Conclusion — what the evidence tells us

The evidence base supports a clear directional conclusion: for the adult amateur beach volleyball population described here, the jump-float serve is a **reasonable-risk target skill** — categorically less threatening to the shoulder than jump-topspin (Reeser 2010 kinetics and epidemiology; confirmed across beach and indoor cohorts) but meaningfully more demanding than standing float. The dominant modifiable risk factors are **prior shoulder injury, scapular dyskinesis, and ER/IR strength imbalance** (De Toledo 2025; Intelangelo 2025) — not raw experience level or age alone. Volume management — session caps, inter-session recovery, ACWR monitoring — is the primary lever an app can pull, because no safe technique eliminates the cumulative-load driver of overuse pathology.

The self-coached unsupervised context changes the defaults rather than the structure. A supervised athlete can push the published ACWR "sweet spot" to its 1.3–1.5 boundary; a self-coached athlete should sit at 1.1–1.3. A supervised athlete can probe to a GIRD threshold of 25°; a self-coached athlete should see a clinician at 20°. The app's most valuable non-obvious feature may not be a rep counter but a **monthly visual scapula-asymmetry check** — infraspinatus atrophy in beach volleyball is common (30% in pros), typically painless, and will be missed by every pain-based monitoring system. Ship that check, ship conservative volume caps, ship the ER/IR strength gate, and ship explicit physio-referral routing — and the product will be defensible alongside the evidence rather than ahead of it.

The single biggest evidence gap requiring post-launch monitoring is the **absence of any validated per-session serve-count threshold in amateur beach players**. Instrumenting the app to collect anonymized rep, recovery, symptom, and PROM data across its user base could contribute the first real-world cohort evidence to the literature — and tighten the numbers that today must be shipped as practitioner extrapolations.
