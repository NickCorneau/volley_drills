## Sand-specific considerations

Sand changes the problem, but it does not automatically make it safe. Overall injury incidence in beach volleyball is lower than indoor volleyball, and beach players appear to have fewer ankle injuries than indoor players, but shoulder overuse remains a major issue. The key takeaway for a serving app is that sand may reduce some peak lower-limb loading, yet it does **not** remove the need to manage repetitive overhead load. citeturn44search1turn35search5turn44search19

The direct biomechanics are fairly consistent. Vertical jumping on sand yields **lower jump height**, **lower vertical ground-reaction force**, **lower power**, and **lower leg stiffness** than rigid surfaces. In one comparison, beach jumps on sand were lower than land jumps because of reduced ground-reaction force; in another, drop jumps on sand showed lower height, lower peak vertical force, lower power, and larger knee flexion in the downward phase. Beach-specific landing behavior matters too. In professional beach volleyball, players landed on **both feet more often on sand than indoor players did on court**, and the softer surface has been proposed as one reason beach athletes show lower tendinopathy rates than indoor athletes. At the same time, one-footed landings still appear after faster, more asymmetric actions, and those are the situations most likely to raise lower-limb load.
