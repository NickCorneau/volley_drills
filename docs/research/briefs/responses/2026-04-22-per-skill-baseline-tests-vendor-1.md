# Baseline skill assessments for amateur beach volleyball: what the evidence actually supports

**Bottom line up front.** Across passing, serving, and setting, **only one peer-reviewed test battery was developed and validated specifically on sand** — Zetou, Giatsis & Tzetzis (2005) — and it is small-sample (n=40), adolescent-skewed (ages 13–26), and requires a partner. Every other candidate that meets modern psychometric standards (Gabbett & Georgieff 2006; Costa et al. 2024) was validated indoors, mostly on junior or college populations. For a courtside mobile app used by 2–5-year recreational adults on sand, the defensible path is a **hybrid: anchor partner-mode tests on Zetou's beach protocols, anchor solo-mode tests on self-administered derivatives (Thissen-Milder bump-set, Costa concentric-ring serve), and explicitly disclose that adult-recreational-amateur-on-sand reliability is a literature gap the app's own data must fill.** The app should expect **test-retest ICCs in the 0.75–0.90 band for partner-mode tests and 0.70–0.85 for solo adaptations**, with self-scoring bias likely compressing that another 10–20% based on athletics-meta-analytic extrapolation (no volleyball-specific self-scoring evidence exists). Serve and attack have the strongest match-outcome signal in beach performance analysis; passing is the hardest to self-administer honestly without a partner.

---

## The landscape in one paragraph

The academic literature on volleyball skill testing is dominated by indoor, coach-administered, scholastic/collegiate tests from 1937–1991 (Bassett-Glassow, French-Cooper, Brady, Russell-Lange, Liba-Stauff, AAHPER/AAHPERD, NCSU/Bartlett), with one Australian modern battery (Gabbett & Georgieff 2006) bringing ICCs up to modern standards (0.85–0.98). Beach-specific peer-reviewed evidence is essentially **one 2005 Greek study (Zetou, Giatsis & Tzetzis) plus a follow-up 2023 line-shot attack validation**. Federations (FIVB, USA Volleyball, Volleyball Canada, AVCA) publish coaching-education curricula and long-term athlete development matrices but **no federation publishes a standardized self-administered baseline test with reliability data for adult recreational beach players**. The Special Olympics Beach Volleyball Skills Assessment Test (BVSAT, 2024) is the single federation-tier quantified protocol, but was designed for divisioning athletes with intellectual disabilities. Practitioner materials (Better at Beach, JVA/Mashima, Volleyball Paradise Club) offer convergent numeric thresholds — 7/10 serves in, 13/20 passes to target, 20 consecutive self pass-sets — but **no reliability evidence at any tier**. The courtside-amateur-self-administered intersection is where the literature thins to nearly nothing.

## Per-skill assessment catalog

### Passing / reception

**1. Zetou, Giatsis & Tzetzis (2005) beach pass test — ★ only peer-reviewed beach-validated pass test.** *Journal of Human Movement Studies* 49(3), 215–230. Athlete stands in back court on a regulation beach court; a partner serves ten balls from behind the opposite endline; athlete forearm-passes each into a setter-target zone near the net. Ordinal scoring aligned to zone proximity (higher points = closer to setter target). **Reliability:** test–retest **r ≈ 0.97 for passing**, confirmed by expert-panel content validity and construct validity discriminating novice/intermediate/expert. Sample n=40, ages 13–26. **Partner required: yes (server).** Equipment: beach court, net, cones/towel for target. **Suitability for amateur beach solo/partner use: HIGH in partner mode; the most ecologically valid candidate in existence.** Caveat: reliability sample is adolescent-skewed; adult-recreational reliability is an empirical gap.

**2. Gabbett & Georgieff (2006) junior skills passing subtest (accuracy only).** *International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance* 1(2), 95–107. DOI: 10.1123/ijspp.1.2.95. Partner tosses standardized balls; player forearm-passes to a setter target near the net across ten trials. **Reliability:** ICC **0.85–0.98** (intratester and intertester), typical error 0.2–10%. Discriminated junior national > state > novice (p<.05). **Partner required: yes.** Equipment: net, target, experienced tosser. **Suitability: MEDIUM**; strong reliability but indoor-validated, and the original protocol includes a coach-rated technique rubric that is not amateur-feasible — drop that half and keep accuracy-only scoring. Beach validity extrapolated.

**3. AAHPERD pass-over-rope test.** AAHPER (1969), *Skills Test Manual: Volleyball for Boys and Girls.* Tosser in opposite zone 2 throws over a rope at 2.24–2.46 m; receiver forearm-passes into a 3×2 m (or 3×4 m) target in their own zone 2. Ten or twenty trials. Ordinal scoring: 2 target / 1 line / 0 miss. **Reliability:** original manual reports test–retest in the 0.80s; Cothran (1992, WKU thesis) found the pass subtest did **not** significantly discriminate game performance — a validity red flag. **Partner required: yes.** Beach validity: none (indoor origin). **Suitability: MEDIUM-LOW** — simple and recognizable but weak discrimination at this population's skill band.

**4. Bartlett et al. (NCSU, 1991) pass item.** *JOPERD* 62(2), 19–21. DOI: 10.1080/07303084.1991.10606554. Partner performs free-ball toss from opposite endline over a 2.40 m rope; receiver passes to front-left setter target. Ordinal target-ring scoring with steep back-edge penalties. **Reliability:** ~0.80 ICC on college PE students (n=313); Costa et al. (2024) critique the scoring cliff at target back edges. **Partner required: yes.** **Suitability: MEDIUM.** Back-edge cliff is a liability for the intermediate population.

**5. Special Olympics BVSAT-Forearm Pass (2024).** Tosser in opposite mid-court delivers ten two-handed overhead tosses (five right-back, five left-back); athlete passes toward a stationary target person 2 m off net, arms raised. Peak-of-arc must exceed net height; zones score 1–5. **Reliability data: none published.** **Partner required: yes (tosser + target).** **Suitability: MEDIUM** — the only federation-tier beach-specific protocol, but designed for divisioning adults with intellectual disabilities, not recreational amateur discrimination.

**Solo-adaptable passing substitute.** Pocek et al. (2023, *J Funct Morphol Kinesiol*, PMC10204436) kinesthetic-differentiation forearm-pass task — self-toss to a target — reports ICC **0.78–0.87** in indoor female players. This is a **platform-control surrogate, not a reception-fidelity test**, because the subject controls ball entry. Acceptable for solo baseline if honestly labeled.

### Serving

**1. Costa, Valentini, Nascimento & Ugrinowitsch (2024) serve accuracy-test — ★ highest modern psychometric rigor.** *Journal of Human Sport and Exercise* 19(2), 570–578. DOI: 10.55860/jj2b1p83. Server 5 m behind endline; concentric 1/2/3/4 m bullseye target 4 m from net on opposite side; triangular in-corridor. Fifteen overhand serves. Graduated 2–22 point scoring (bullseye 22, decreasing by 2 per ring; in-corridor-outside-target 14; net-top-band 12; out-of-court 6). **Reliability:** ICC **0.81 novices / 0.84 experienced / 0.91 total**; SEM 2.58–4.60%. Content validity CVC = 0.93. Cohen's d experienced-vs-novice = 2.20. Cluster analysis correctly reclassifies 90.9%. **Partner required: no (scorer optional, phone video works).** Beach validity extrapolated but the ring geometry ports naturally to sand (tape/cones). **Suitability: HIGH.** Graduated scoring avoids the NCSU back-edge cliff — the single best match for a 2–5-year recreational population where neither floor nor ceiling should compress variance.

**2. Zetou, Giatsis & Tzetzis (2005) beach serve test.** Same paper as above. Ten serves into beach-court zones, ordinal zone scoring. **Reliability:** reported as "adequate" with beach-specific expert-panel content validity; exact ICC behind paywall but cited in the 0.80s–0.90s range. **Partner required: no** (scorer optional). **Suitability: HIGH** as the only beach-native zone geometry, but use with Costa's ring scoring for finer discrimination.

**3. Brumbach Volleyball Service Test (~1967).** Brumbach, W.B., *Beginning Volleyball: A Syllabus for Teachers.* Ten serves must pass low under a rope ~8 ft above net on the server's side and land in deep-court zones. **Reliability:** grey-literature; Cothran (1992) found Brumbach Serve was the **#1 discriminating variable** (standardized discriminant coefficient 0.917, p<.0001) separating game-play ability groups. **Partner required: no** (scorer). Equipment: rope + standards — **awkward on sand** without extra rigging. **Suitability: MEDIUM** — strongest discrimination in the classical literature but rope setup is not courtside-feasible.

**4. AAHPER(D) Serve Test (1969/1984).** Ten serves from behind endline into zones worth 1–4 or 1–5. **Reliability:** validity r ≈ 0.70 vs expert ratings; test–retest r ≈ 0.80+. **Partner required: no.** **Suitability: MEDIUM** — widely cited, simple, but coarse zone scoring suffers ceiling compression with experienced players (Costa 2024 critique). Indoor-extrapolated.

**5. Gabbett & Georgieff (2006) serve subtest.** Binary in/out scoring across serves. **Reliability:** ICC part of the 0.85–0.98 battery range; sensitive to 8-week training changes. **Partner required: no.** **Suitability: MEDIUM-HIGH** as a consistency complement to a placement test — binary scoring is the simplest possible self-score.

**Pressure/fatigue overlay.** Lidor et al. (2007, *JSCR* 21(3), 937–942. DOI: 10.1519/R-19455.1) serve-after-exertion protocol adds block–dig–approach reps between serves. Beach-extrapolatable because sand amplifies fatigue; no additional equipment. Optional add-on, not a standalone test.

**Serve type differentiation.** No peer-reviewed test **separately validates** scoring for float, jump-float, and jump-topspin. Performance analysis (López-Martínez & Palao 2009, 2020; Buscà et al. 2012 *J Sports Sci* 30(3):269–276; Koch & Tilp 2009) shows elite women's beach is dominated by float variants (jump-float 44.8%, standing float 32.8%, power-jump 22.3% efficacy; Koch & Tilp report ~80% floats overall), while men's beach uses more jump-topspin. **Implication:** tag serve-type as metadata, apply identical placement scoring, stratify outputs — but label type-specific benchmarks as practitioner-derived.

**Disqualified for courtside app use:** Palao & Valadés (2009, *Strength Cond J* 31(6):47–51; 2012, *Biology of Sport* 29(4):281–284) radar-gun serve-speed protocols.

### Setting

**1. Zetou, Giatsis & Tzetzis (2005) beach set test — ★ only peer-reviewed beach-validated set test.** Ball is fed by partner or consistent launch; athlete delivers overhead set (bump-set permitted) to a target representing attack location — 0.5–1 m off net, 2–2.5 m above net, 2 m from setter position. Ten trials across two days. **Reliability:** Pearson **r ≈ 0.85–0.95** test–retest between days. **Partner required: yes (feeder).** **Suitability: HIGH in partner mode.** The only peer-reviewed option that models the beach-specific setter→hitter geometry.

**2. Gabbett & Georgieff (2006) setting subtest.** Coach toss to setter; setter delivers through a **1.5 m × 1 m target window** at a hitter-relevant distance. Ten trials. **Reliability:** ICC **0.85–0.98**, typical error 0.2–10%. **Partner required: yes.** Hand-set only. **Suitability: MEDIUM-HIGH** — best modern reliability in the literature, but indoor-validated and the feeder must be consistent.

**3. Thissen-Milder & Mayhew (1991) self bump-set test.** *J Sports Med Phys Fitness* 31(3), 380–384. PMID: 1798309. Player alternately bumps and sets to self continuously within a 15×15 ft area above a height criterion; count of valid consecutive cycles. **Reliability:** "adequate for classification"; correctly classified **78%** of starters vs non-starters in a high-school sample. Exact ICC unpublished. **Partner required: no — fully solo.** **Suitability: HIGH for solo mode** — this is effectively the same protocol as Better at Beach's "20 consecutive pass-and-set" Level 3 test and Special Olympics BVSAT-Bump-Set, giving practitioner-convergence across three independent sources.

**4. Liba & Stauff (1963) two-rope set test.** *Research Quarterly* 34, 56–63. Overhead pass must clear ropes at 11 ft and 13 ft and land in a floor target; score = height × distance product. **Reliability:** r ≈ 0.80. **Partner required: typically (tosser).** **Suitability: MEDIUM.** Conceptually the best-matched classical test because it scores both **arc height and placement simultaneously** — precisely what a beach hand-set needs — but rope setup is not courtside-feasible.

**5. Brady / AAHPER wall-volley family.** Continuous overhead volleys against a wall target for 30–60 s. **Reliability:** Brady original r ≈ 0.86 validity, 0.93 split-half; Russell-Lange r ≈ 0.82–0.87; AAHPER wall-volley discriminated game performance in Cothran (1992). **Partner required: no.** **Suitability for beach: LOW** because beach courts typically have no suitable wall. Useful only if the app explicitly adds an off-sand home-practice mode.

**Critical gaps in setting literature.** (a) **No peer-reviewed test evaluates hand-set cleanness** per FIVB beach rule 9.2 (no rotation, simultaneous contact) — this is the exact discriminator between legal and illegal beach sets and is uniquely important for amateur progression. (b) **No validated bump-set-only protocol exists.** (c) No setting test captures decision-making (hitter read).

## Recommendations: what the app should ship

### Passing
**Primary (partner mode): Zetou 2005 beach pass, adapted.** Ten partner-served balls; pass into a cone-marked 2×3 m zone near the net; score each attempt 0/1/2 (miss / in-line / in-zone) for a 0–20 total. This is the only protocol with beach-specific reliability evidence, and the three-level ordinal scale maps onto the 0–3 Coleman pass rating familiar to coached players. **Compromise:** requires a partner; reliability evidence is adolescent-skewed. **Disclose:** "based on Zetou et al. 2005 (r≈0.97, n=40 ages 13–26); adult recreational reliability is unknown and will be estimated from your own app data."

**Secondary (solo mode): modified Pocek 2023 self-toss to target (platform-control surrogate).** Ten self-tosses; forearm-pass to target 3 m away and 2 m high. Label it honestly as a **platform-control measure, not a reception test** — because the user controls ball entry, ecological validity is compromised. Use only when no partner is available. **Compromise:** loses serve-reception validity; ICC likely 0.75–0.85 on sand.

### Serving
**Primary: Costa et al. 2024 graduated-ring serve accuracy test, adapted.** Ten overhand serves from behind the endline toward a concentric ring target 3–4 m from the net on the opposite side (sand-tape or cones at 1/2/3/4 m radii). Graduated scoring 2–22 per attempt. Solo-administrable. ICC **0.81 novice / 0.84 experienced / 0.91 total**; Cohen's d = 2.20 discriminating novice from experienced undergraduates — the population most similar to the app's users. **Compromise:** indoor-validated, extrapolated to sand; ring marking requires ~30 s setup. Pair with the **Zetou 2005 beach-court zone geometry** so the app's output maps to standard beach rating language (zones 1/4/5 deep, short zone, line vs angle).

**Secondary: Gabbett 2006-style binary consistency counter.** Twenty serves, count % in-court. Ultra-simple self-score, ICC part of the 0.85–0.98 range. Run as a quick "consistency check" mode or warmup baseline. **Compromise:** binary scoring compresses variance and ignores placement.

**Optional pressure block:** Lidor 2007 exertion overlay (two approach-jumps + two defensive moves between serves) for users who want a fatigue-adjusted score. Peer-reviewed and equipment-free on sand.

### Setting
**Primary (partner mode): Zetou 2005 beach set, adapted.** Ten partner-fed balls; athlete delivers overhead set to target at 0.5–1 m off net, 2–2.5 m high, 2 m away. Ordinal zone scoring. **Compromise:** partner consistency is a confound; does not assess cleanness/legality per beach rule 9.2 (add a practitioner-derived Trinsey-style 6-point rubric for cleanness but label "practitioner-derived, not peer-validated").

**Primary (solo mode): Thissen-Milder self bump-set.** Alternate bump and set to self continuously within ~15×15 ft, ball above net-height each cycle. Count consecutive legal contacts; one 60–90 s trial × 2–3 repeats. This is **the single most-converged solo protocol in the literature**: peer-reviewed (Thissen-Milder 1991 classified 78% of starters/non-starters), federation-published (Special Olympics BVSAT-Bump-Set, max 50), and practitioner-endorsed (Better at Beach Level 3 = 20 consecutive). **Compromise:** exact ICC unpublished; assesses ball control rather than set-to-hitter delivery.

### Cross-cutting dose
Use **6–10 scored trials + 1–2 familiarization trials** per skill. This sits in the defensible reliability sweet spot across Zetou 2005 (10), Costa 2024 (15), Gabbett 2006 (6–10), and Seliga/Melo et al. 2022 (2 trials sufficient after 1 familiarization, BMC Pediatrics). For continuous counts (pass-to-self, bump-set self-cycle), use **two 30-s trials** and record the better score.

## Cross-cutting methodology notes

**Self-scoring bias.** No volleyball-specific evidence exists. Adjacent athletics meta-analytic evidence (Mabe & West 1982 reporting r ≈ 0.47 between self and objective performance — the highest of any domain; Dunning et al. 2004 *Psychol Sci Pub Interest* 5(3):69–106) predicts **self-score–observer agreement around r = 0.40–0.50, kappa around 0.30–0.60**. Lower-skill users directionally overestimate (Dunning-Kruger, McIntosh et al. 2022 *R Soc Open Sci*). **Mitigation:** use externally-defined concrete criteria (ball landed in cone-marked zone) rather than technique Likert ratings; record via phone camera when possible; include a 30-second calibration tutorial showing what each score level looks like (Aguayo-Albasini et al. 2024 *BMC Psychology* showed calibration lifted inter-rater ICC to 0.94). **Do not ship subjective technique self-ratings** — they will dominate error variance.

**Environmental effects.** Sand reduces jump height ~5–15% vs rigid surfaces (Giatsis et al. 2018 *J Sports Sci* 36(9):997–1008; Panoutsakopoulos et al. 2023). FIVB sand specs (40 cm depth, 0.25–1 mm grain) are rarely met on recreational courts — Kasprzak & Łopuch (2022, *Applied Sciences* 12(14):6985) found **zero of eleven tested Wrocław courts met full FIVB standards.** No peer-reviewed quantitative estimate exists of wind's effect on pass/serve accuracy; practitioner consensus puts material degradation at Beaufort 3+ (~12 km/h). FIVB heat-stress monitoring uses WBGT >32°C thresholds (Bahr & Reeser 2012 *BJSM*). **App implications:** capture wind (weather API), sand condition (1-tap loose/firm/wet), and time-of-day; flag unusable conditions (wind ≥ Beaufort 3); never compare scores across conditions without a within-player baseline.

**Rubric type.** No volleyball head-to-head comparison exists. General measurement literature (Hallgren 2012 *Tutor Quant Methods Psychol* 8(1):23–34; Grant et al. 2017 *Educ Psychol Meas*) supports **continuous > ordinal > binary** for reliability and discrimination, because partial-credit information is preserved and the "kappa paradox" under prevalence imbalance is avoided. **Recommend:** continuous distance-from-target when phone-vision can measure it; ordinal 0–4 zones as default; binary in/out only for edge tests (court in/out).

**Beach performance analysis — what actually matters.** Convergent evidence across Giatsis & Zahariadis (2008 *IJPAS* 8(1):31–43), Palao & Ortega (2015), Medeiros et al. (2017), Papadopoulou et al. (2020), and Buscà et al. (2012) establishes **attack efficacy and serve efficacy as the top two discriminators of winning vs losing beach teams**, followed by reception and setting. Elite ace rates ~2.9–5.1%; serve errors ~7.7–8.3%. This suggests the app's serve and attack modules carry the most match-outcome signal — and both are self-administrable; passing and blocking are inherently partner-dependent.

## Gaps and open questions

Five gaps would materially affect post-launch calibration and are not answered by the literature:

First, **no generalizability-theory decomposition of person × trial × day × rater × environment variance exists for any beach volleyball skill test.** This means the app cannot answer "how many trials do I need to detect a true skill change of 10%?" from published sources. The app should log every trial and run its own G-study once N allows.

Second, **no study has measured self-score vs video-score agreement magnitude in unsupervised amateur volleyball testing.** The 10–20% self-score inflation assumption is extrapolated from Mabe & West's 1982 athletics meta-analysis. A prospective validation — users score themselves, app re-scores via video — would close this gap cheaply.

Third, **no normative data exists for adult recreational (2–5 yr experience) beach players on any validated test.** All published norms are from juniors, college students, or Greek adolescents. The app must generate its own percentiles and explicitly warn users that external norms don't apply.

Fourth, **no peer-reviewed test assesses hand-set cleanness per FIVB beach rule 9.2**, which is the exact discriminator between "intermediate" and "advanced" beach setters. This is a literature-level gap. Any cleanness feature must be labeled practitioner-derived.

Fifth, **no serve-type-specific psychometrics exist** for float vs jump-float vs jump-topspin accuracy scoring. Performance-analysis efficacy data exist but per-trial reliability does not. Stratification is ecological, not validated.

**Study designs that would resolve these, in order of ROI:** (a) a G-study of 60–80 adult recreational beach players, each performing each candidate test 3× over 2 weeks under logged conditions — closes gaps 1, 3, and partially 5 in a single 6-month data collection; (b) a self-score vs video-score agreement study within the G-study (closes gap 2); (c) a cleanness-rubric expert-panel content-validity study with blinded referee scoring on slow-motion video (closes gap 4).

---

## Conclusion

The honest answer for the product designer is: **strong evidence exists for serving (Costa 2024 for ring-target placement, Zetou 2005 for beach zone geometry), moderate evidence for partner-mode passing and setting (Zetou 2005, Gabbett 2006), and weak evidence — practitioner-convergent but not psychometrically validated — for solo-mode setting (Thissen-Milder / BVSAT / Better at Beach) and solo-mode passing (Pocek self-toss, labeled as a platform-control surrogate only).** The recommended app baseline ships one partner-mode and one solo-mode variant per skill, uses continuous scoring captured by phone video where feasible, logs environmental covariates, and treats its first 10,000 user-sessions as the dataset that will finally generate adult-recreational-on-sand norms the published literature has never produced. Expect post-launch surprises in three places: self-scoring bias will be larger than athletics-meta-analytic estimates (because beach amateurs have no indoor judges shaping self-calibration), single-session scores will be noisier than indoor test-retest ICCs suggest, and the serve test will discriminate users better than the passing and setting tests — reflecting both the underlying performance-analysis signal and the unavoidable partner-dependency of reception and delivery. The product should be honest about all three from day one.
