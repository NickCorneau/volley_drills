# Baseline skill assessments for amateur beach volleyball

## Executive conclusion

The literature does **not** give you a clean, beach-specific, fully self-administered answer for all three skills. The strongest bottom-line finding is that **serving has the best psychometric support**, mostly from target-based accuracy tests. **Passing and setting are much thinner**, and the most established tests are either indoor-derived, partner-administered, wall-based, or validated in populations that do not match your target user. I found **one beach-specific study** that explicitly built tests for **serve, pass, and set** and reported that they were valid and reliable for beach volleyball players, but the accessible abstract does not expose the exact coefficients or full scoring geometry; that means it is useful as a signal that beach-specific testing exists, but not enough by itself to ship a product implementation without obtaining the full paper. citeturn53search0turn53search10turn53search16

If the app must ship now, the most defensible product stance is asymmetric. Ship a **serve baseline** confidently. Ship **pass** and **set** only when the player has either a partner or a wall, and be explicit that those baselines are adapted from indoor or general-volleyball evidence rather than validated specifically for adult amateur beach players on sand. If you force a solo/no-wall/no-partner baseline for pass and set, you will mostly be measuring **ball-control proxies**, not reception or setting in a way that is ecologically close to actual beach side-out play. citeturn52view1turn17view0turn36search1turn30view0turn31view0

A second practical conclusion is that short tests are possible, but not one-shot tests. Across the field tests I found, the minimum defensible dose is usually **6 to 10 scored repetitions**, or a **30-second wall sequence repeated two to three times**. Historical wall-volley work shows reliability improves materially as repetitions increase; one old repeated-volley variant was too noisy at 10 hits but improved to **0.83** reliability at 20 hits, and classic serve/pass/set batteries usually used **10 trials** per subtest. For your app, that argues for very short, highly standardized outcome tests with visible targets and simple line-based scoring, not technique checklists. citeturn38view0turn17view0turn34view0

## Passing and reception assessments

For passing/reception, the best beach-specific signal is the 2005 beach-volleyball paper by Zetou, Giatsis, and Tzetzis. Its accessible abstract says the authors constructed beach-specific tests for **set, pass, and serve** after accounting for the "special characteristics of the game," used **40 beach volleyball players**, and concluded the tests were valid and reliable for evaluating the ability to set, pass, and serve effectively. That is valuable because it confirms the problem has been tackled in the beach context rather than simply assumed from indoor evidence. The drawback is that the accessible abstract does **not** expose the actual scoring protocol or the reliability coefficients, so for implementation it is a "high face-validity, incomplete psychometric transparency" source unless you acquire the full text. I would rate it **high concept relevance, medium implementation readiness, unknown coefficient transparency**. citeturn53search0turn53search10turn53search16

The most commonly reusable partner-based passing baseline is the **NCSU Volleyball Skills Test Battery** forearm-pass test. In a widely used secondary description of the battery, the athlete receives **10 tossed balls**, five from the left and five from the right, and forearm-passes them over a **3.20 m rope** into target zones scored **1 to 5**, with **0** for balls that do not clear the rope or miss the court; the same source reports reliability coefficients of **0.73** for forearm passing, **0.65** for serving, and **0.88** for overhand passing/setting. For your target use case, this test has a lot going for it: it is quick, strongly outcome-based, and easy to score honestly. But it is still **partner-dependent**, indoor-derived, and more about **directed first-contact accuracy** than about reading an actual serve in wind. I would rate it **medium reliability, high practicality with partner, low solo-adaptability**. citeturn17view0

An AAHPERD-style underhand-pass test offers a similar structure. In a modern outdoor implementation of the AAHPERD subtests, participants received a tossed ball, passed it over a **2.24 m rope**, and tried to land it in a **3 m × 2 m target** for **4 points** or on the boundary for **2 points**; participants performed **10 trials**. In that outdoor study, the underhand-pass subtest produced **Cronbach's alpha = 0.812**, which is acceptable for field use, while the overhand-pass subtest was **0.774** and the serve subtest **0.678**. That paper used beginners on an outdoor court, not beach adults, so it is still an extrapolation. But it does matter that the test worked outside the lab and outside the indoor gym. I would rate it **medium reliability, medium ecological fit, low solo-adaptability because a feeder is needed**. citeturn19view0turn20view1

A fourth usable data point is the forearm-pass subtest from the **Volleyball Skills Assessment Test** developed for athletes in a entity["organization","Special Olympics International","multisport nonprofit"] setting. That battery reported **test–retest reliability above .80** for all subtests except the female set subtest (**.75**), and concurrent validity against judges' ratings was very high for pass (**r = .94**) and serve (**r = .93**). The problem is obvious: the target population is not your user population, and the battery's overall predictive validity for team pooling was questioned. I would use VSAT as evidence that simple field skill tests **can** be reliable, not as a directly transferable beach-amateur baseline. citeturn22search4turn22search9

What these pass tests measure is mostly **first-contact outcome accuracy**, not platform shape, not movement economy, and not tactical decision-making. None of the located pass baselines meaningfully incorporate beach-specific complications like serve float, wind drift, sun glare, or choosing between a hand receive and platform receive. That is the core literature gap for your product. citeturn17view0turn19view0turn53search16

## Serving assessments

Serving is where the literature is best. The strongest psychometric option I found is the 2024 overhand **volleyball serve accuracy-test** study from researchers at entity["organization","Federal University of Minas Gerais","Brazilian university"]. The test used a bull's-eye style floor target on the far court, scored serves from **2 to 22 points**, established content validity with experts, face validity with coaches/teachers/athletes, and reported **ICC = .81** for novices, **.84** for experienced players, and **.91** for the total sample. It also showed large experience-group differences (**Cohen's d = 2.20** at test; **1.59** at retest) and correctly classified **90.9%** of cases by cluster analysis. For a product designer, that is very useful: it is short, target-based, sensitive across lower levels, and has the best reported reliability numbers of anything I found for a serve baseline. The compromise is that it is **not beach-specific**, does not distinguish jump-float from jump-topspin, and uses a fixed target geometry that is more "serve accuracy" than "serve pressure." citeturn52view1turn52view2turn51view0

The older **AAHPERD serve test** remains one of the reference points in the field. A modern outdoor implementation describes the server standing in a **2 m central area** and making **10 serves** into rectangular zones worth **1 to 4 points**, with more rear and lateral areas scoring higher. In the same outdoor study, the service subtest's internal consistency was only **α = 0.678**, clearly weaker than the pass and set subtests. The test is easy to understand and can be self-scored with line-based honesty, but its psychometric weakness is exactly why the newer bull's-eye test exists. I would rate AAHPERD serving **low-to-medium reliability, medium amateur usability, high simplicity**. citeturn19view0turn20view1turn18search12

The **NCSU serve test** is also common, but I would treat it cautiously. In the same secondary description used above, the athlete serves **10 times** into court areas marked **2, 3, and 4**, with **0** for misses; if the ball lands on a boundary, the higher score is awarded. The same source reports **0.65** reliability for serving, which is merely fair. The newer Costa paper explicitly criticizes this family of tests for sensitivity, noting that the "maximum-score/back-edge" logic can punish an experienced player who misses by a centimetre while allowing novices to accumulate low-but-safe points, which may flatten improvement detection. That is exactly the kind of noise you do not want in a calibration baseline. citeturn17view0turn51view0

If you want the literature to distinguish serve types, the best field-test evidence I found is from the 2023 **kinesthetic differentiation** work on standing float serves. That study tested three float-service variants: **with a net** (**ICC = 0.66**), **without a net** (**ICC = 0.77**), and **from 6 m off the net** (**ICC = 0.87**). In each version, athletes first hit **three maximal serves**, then made **six scored attempts** to a personalized target derived from **50% or 75%** of their maximum distance. These are more like "distance-control" baselines than tactical placement tests, but they are short and objective. For recreational beach adults who mostly use a standing float or jump-float, the **FST6m** and the no-net float version are credible templates. For jump-topspin, I did **not** find a validated, self-administered amateur field test without video, radar, or an observer. citeturn39view1turn39view2turn38view0

The literature on "pressure" serving is less encouraging than it sounds. In the study often cited for this point, players performed a target-based service test in rested and post-exertion conditions, but the data showed **no differences between rested and exertion conditions** and **no differences between teams** in service performance. That makes the fatigue-loaded variant a weak choice for your baseline layer: it adds hassle without clearly improving discrimination. citeturn14search17

There is also a practical beach-volleyball benchmark document that is useful even though it is not a validation paper. A federation-style beach baseline document lists separate serve proficiencies for **location**, **jump top**, **jump float**, and **wind adjustment** across age/ability tiers. That is important product guidance: the beach world itself treats **wind adjustment** as a first-class serve skill, but the psychometric literature has not yet caught up with a robust amateur test for it. citeturn48view0

## Setting assessments

Setting is the hardest skill to assess cleanly for your constraints. Again, the beach-specific Zetou/Giatsis/Tzetzis paper matters because it explicitly says it built a **beach set test** alongside pass and serve. But without the accessible protocol and coefficients, it is more a signpost than a ready-made product implementation. I would absolutely try to obtain that full paper before locking any beach-setting baseline. citeturn53search0turn53search16

Among tests with visible procedures and actual numbers, the best partner-based setting baseline is the **NCSU overhand pass/setting** test. The athlete stands in the attack area, receives a ball from a designated feeder, and sets the ball over a **4 m rope** into **1-to-5** scoring zones. The same secondary source reports **0.88** reliability for this overhand-pass test, the strongest of the classic NCSU subtests. For a beach app, this is the cleanest partner-available setting candidate because it maps to what a setter is supposed to do: create a precise, hittable ball with height and direction. The compromise is obvious: it is indoor-derived, does not capture beach hand-set legality under strict officiating, and requires a partner. citeturn17view0

If a wall is available, the strongest solo-friendly hand-setting test I found is Harner's 1982 **two-wall overhand passing test** from entity["organization","Eastern Illinois University","public university"]. The athlete alternates overhand passes between two wall targets set at right angles for **30 seconds**, with **three trials**; the best scoring rule yielded **reliability = .87** and **validity = .8274** against coach ratings. For pure self-administration, this is excellent. For actual beach-setting validity, it is mixed. It measures hand control, rhythm, and directional precision, but not setting to a hitter in wind, not legal beach hand contact under match scrutiny, and not bump-setting. Still, if your app supports a "wall mode," this is a very good candidate. citeturn36search1

The classic **Russell–Lange volleying test** is another solo proxy. The athlete tosses the ball to a wall and volleys above a marked net-height line for **30 seconds** from behind a **3-foot restraining line**; the best of three trials is recorded. Historical reviews report reliability of **.87** for the best of three trials and **.90** for the sum of three, with validity correlations ranging **.63 to .80** in junior-high girls. That is respectable for a simple wall test. But it measures **generic volleying ability**, not beach setting specifically, and it is too ecologically broad to be a primary beach-setting baseline unless paired with a more specific target task. citeturn30view0turn34view0

**Brady's Wall Volleys** is even older and even broader, but the psychometrics are strong: the original wall-volley test reported **test–retest reliability = 0.92** and **validity = 0.86**, with modified female versions around **0.83** reliability and **0.70** validity. It is one minute of consecutive legal wall volleys to a target area. The reason I would not make Brady your primary setting baseline is not reliability; it is specificity. It is really a **general volleying/ball-control** test, not a beach set test. The psychometric history is strong; the ecological validity for beach setting is limited. citeturn31view0turn34view0

The AAHPERD-style overhand pass subtest sits between those extremes. In the outdoor beginner study, the athlete received a tossed ball and passed over a rope into one of two targets; the overhand-pass subtest showed **α = 0.774**. That is workable, but weaker than NCSU's reported **0.88** and less setting-specific than the two-wall test's direct hand-setting focus. My reading is simple: if a player has a partner, use the **NCSU-style partner target set**; if solo and wall-available, use **Harner**; if solo and no wall, there is no beach-valid option I would call strong. citeturn19view0turn20view1turn17view0turn36search1

A separate note on **bump-setting**: I did **not** locate a validated beach-amateur bump-set-only baseline with usable psychometrics. The federation-style beach benchmark document does distinguish **bump sets** from **hand sets** in its proficiency expectations, which supports the product decision to separate them in the UI, but that is a benchmark document, not a validation study. citeturn48view0

My recommendation is blunt.

For **serve**, ship a single target-based accuracy baseline now. If you want the safest evidence choice, use a simplified version of the **Costa** test: visible target rings, simple ordinal scoring, and outcome-only assessment. If you want stronger beach flavor for standing float servers, offer an alternate **float-service target** mode modeled on the **no-net** or **6 m** kinesthetic tests, but label it as a float-serve calibration test rather than a general serve-grade. For **pass**, do **not** pretend there is a strong solo/no-wall beach-valid baseline if there is not. If a partner is available, use a **NCSU/AAHPERD-style forearm pass to target** with **10 scored balls** and a dead-simple ordinal rubric. ## Gaps, bias, and implementation cautions

The largest gap is the one you suspected: **beach-specific, adult, self-administered, short-duration skill baselines are sparse**. The beach-specific paper I found is promising but opaque in the accessible abstract, while most psychometric work either comes from indoor/general volleyball or from populations that do not match your user. Later beach validation work I located focused on attack skills rather than pass, serve, or set. That means any product claim stronger than "evidence-informed adaptation" would be overstated for pass and set. citeturn53search0turn22search2

Self-scoring bias is also a real concern, and the sport-specific evidence is weak. In the literature I located, validation studies generally used **researchers, coaches, or judges** to score outcomes; I did **not** locate a paper quantifying **self-vs-observer agreement** for amateur volleyball skill tests. The broader self-assessment literature is not reassuring: one review reported an average self-assessment correlation of about **0.29** with external standards overall and about **0.47** for athletics, which is better but still far from "trustworthy by default." Athletic psychology work also shows athletes often overweight their best moments: in one baseball/softball study, **81.3%** of players in one sample and **76.9%** in another judged their best game as more representative of their true ability than their worst game, and **50%** of those choosing their best game picked a perfect performance as most representative. That supports a strong product rule: **never use self-rated technique quality as the primary baseline score** when you can use visible landing zones, counts, or wall hits instead. citeturn44view0turn45view2turn45view0

Environmental variation is not a side issue in beach volleyball. The official heat-stress protocol used in the professional beach game treats **WBGT** as the relevant composite measure because it captures **temperature, humidity, wind, and solar radiation**, and it explicitly notes that ordinary meteorological-site data can **underestimate courtside conditions**. Beach rules and casebook material also recognize that **wind can materially alter ball flight**, including on sets. In practical terms, I would not try to mathematically "normalize" beach baseline scores for wind or heat at launch. I would do something simpler and more robust: record environment metadata and standardize collection conditions. Same court, same balls, same net height, same approximate time of day, same serve direction, and a simple wind tag such as calm / mild cross / strong cross / into wind / with wind. Then compare players mostly to **their own prior tests under similar conditions**. citeturn48view1turn47search3turn48view0

The obvious study design to close the product gap is a small, pragmatic reliability study in your exact target population: adults with **2–5 years** of recreational beach experience, testing on actual sand, no coach present, phone-guided instructions, and honest self-scoring. The design should compare **self-score vs observer score**, estimate **test–retest ICC**, **SEM**, and **minimal detectable change**, and stratify by context: solo/wall, solo/net, and partner-fed. For pass and set, I would also test whether a "defer until partner present" workflow outperforms a universal solo proxy in predictive utility for session calibration. That is the shortest path from "reasonable adaptation" to "defensible product measurement." citeturn52view1turn36search1turn17view0
