# Jump-Float Serve — Safe Introduction Protocols For Self-Coached Adult Amateurs

## Agent Quick Scan

- **Three-vendor coverage (all 2026-04-22, Moderate confidence).** Vendors 1 and 2 converge on a ~30–45 reps / session cap (vendor 1: 3 × 10–15 = 30–45; vendor 2 balanced: 40; vendor 2 conservative: 25). **Vendor 3 diverges sharply and more conservatively: 3 × 4 = 12 total for first-exposure, progressing to 4 × 4 = 16 only after two clean sessions.** All three agree on ≥48 h between sessions and the bow-and-arrow + in-front-contact + rigid-hand cue triplet.
- **Ship-ready courtside spec for `d36 Jump Float Introduction` (revised downward by vendor 3's evidence).** **3 sets of 4 = 12 reps per session** for the first two sessions, **4 sets of 4 = 16 reps** from session 3 onward if prior two were clean. 20 s enforced timer between reps. 3 min enforced timer between sets (compromise across vendors). **≤2 sessions/week, ≥48 h apart.** This is a change from the prior vendor-1-only spec of 3 × 12 = 36; per our own reconciliation principle (more conservative wins on safety), vendor 3's 12-rep first-exposure anchor takes precedence for a drill literally named "Jump Float Introduction." The 36-rep target is preserved as a later-wave **steady-state drill** (not `d36` itself).
- **Vendor 3's conservative-anchor evidence (worth internalizing).** (a) An **87-serve jump-float fatigue study** in trained D-I players produced clinically meaningful infraspinatus fatigue that **athletes did not reliably perceive** — the direct basis for "don't trust self-reported fatigue alone." (b) Hurd 2009 IHP's **early-phase 4–6 serves per set** structure, which vendor 3 uses as the direct template for first-exposure volume rather than as a lower-bound reference. (c) A **2025 prospective volleyball cohort** with prior-injury OR **25.3** for shoulder pain and scapular-dyskinesis OR **4.2** — strongest quantitative risk-factor signal any vendor cited.
- **Prerequisite gate (Moderate confidence; vendors diverge on depth).** Vendor 1 ships a 2-question gate (wall slide + pain/history). Vendor 2 ships a deeper 3-test gate (pain provocation + active flexion ≥160° + ER/IR ratio ≥0.75). Vendor 3 agrees with vendor 1's minimum but adds a **mandatory 8–10 min volleyball-specific warm-up requirement** before jump-float unlocks in session flow (VolleyVeilig RCT: 21 % acute-injury reduction). **Recommended reconciliation:** vendor 1's 2-question gate for module-unlock; warm-up requirement as a session-builder concern; vendor 2's ER/IR + flexion as optional monthly post-unlock re-checks.
- **Top-3 biomechanical cues (High confidence, all three vendors agree).** Bow-and-arrow arm pre-load / arm loaded early; low in-front toss (don't chase the toss); rigid flat-hand contact with the heel of the palm. Vendor 2 adds a fourth cue (120–130° abduction, avoid hyper-horizontal-adduction) — track for a later-wave `d36` revision. Vendor 3 adds a fifth, beach-specific cue: **jump mostly up-and-down on sand, land balanced on two feet** (rooted in Tilp 2013 beach-landing evidence).
- **Vendor 3-only additions that should ship.** (a) **Mandatory warm-up before unlock** (session-builder concern; ties into any future warm-up prerequisite). (b) **"Slide jump-float" as an explicit intermediate progression step** (Kessel/USAV: quick self-set, minimal toss, arm pre-loaded — a defensible step between stand-float mastery and full-approach jump-float). (c) **Hurd soreness rules as warning-sign copy**: "if sore during warm-up and not gone within first 15 balls, stop and take 2 days off; if soreness >1 h post-session or next-day, step down." (d) **"Do not combine jump-float with jump-topspin in same session"** — forward-compatible constraint; not currently binding because no jump-topspin drill exists.
- **Vendor-2-only additions still on the roadmap** (unchanged from the prior two-vendor state). (a) **Monthly visual scapula-asymmetry photo check** for infraspinatus atrophy (Lajtai 2009, 30 % prevalence, typically painless). (b) **ACWR ≤ 1.3 weekly monitoring**. (c) **Annual 4–6 weeks off-overhead**. (d) **Validated PROMs (KJOC, FAST)** with licensing flagged as do-not-ship-without-physio-input.
- **Categorical do-not-ship (all three vendors agree + each-vendor expansion).** Prior shoulder dislocation / subluxation without cleared rehab; symptomatic SLAP / rotator-cuff tendinopathy. Vendor 2 adds: prior shoulder surgery; Beighton ≥ 5/9 + prior instability; visible infraspinatus atrophy / unexplained ER weakness; active numbness/paresthesia; age ≥ 50 without overhead-throwing history. Vendor 3 escalates prior-injury gate language: "OR 25.3 for shoulder pain in the 2025 cohort" = the clearest do-not-ship signal in the entire literature.
- **GIRD threshold — vendors diverge, and vendor 3 complicates.** Vendor 1: 10–15° side-to-side TRM discrepancy. Vendor 2: IR deficit ≥ 18–20° with TRM deficit ≥ 5°, flag > 25° as urgent. **Vendor 3 adds a twist: the recent volleyball prospective cohort did NOT find a significant direct association between GIRD and pain** — suggesting the whole GIRD self-test layer may be lower-signal than the other gates for volleyball specifically. **Reconciliation:** keep 20° IR deficit as the optional self-test threshold; deprioritize it below the wall-slide + pain/history + warm-up gates; flag threshold language as "do not ship without physio input."
- **Confidence posture.** Volume cap is **Moderate** (all three vendors explicitly label their numbers as extrapolations, not validated thresholds). Cue ranking is High across all three. Prerequisite-gate self-administration is Moderate at best (Bahr 2016 BJSM: "screening tests flag deficits, do not 'clear' athletes"). Vendor 3 is most explicit: "confidence that 12 is safer than 3 × 15 is Moderate; confidence that 12 is the true injury threshold is Low."
- **Single biggest gap (all three vendors agree).** No peer-reviewed study has quantified jump-float shoulder kinetics as a distinct category (only "jump serve" pooled, typically jump-topspin), and no prospective dose-response study exists in amateur self-coached beach players. Vendor 3 reframes this as a product opportunity: **the app can instrument rep count, shank rate, next-day posterior shoulder pain, and error-clustering by rep position — and become the evidence the literature lacks.**

## Executive conclusion

For a self-coached adult amateur introducing the jump-float serve, three vendors have returned and their reconciliation is non-trivial because they materially disagree on ship volume. Vendors 1 and 2 converge on ~30–45 reps per session as the balanced default. **Vendor 3 materially disputes this** with three distinct evidence anchors and recommends 3 × 4 = 12 reps as the first-exposure default. Per our own stated reconciliation principle (more conservative wins on safety questions), and because `d36` is literally authored as an **introduction** drill, the synthesis resolves toward vendor 3's first-exposure spec. The result is a five-layer ship architecture:

1. **A graduated volume cap with enforced pacing (revised from prior vendor-1-only spec).** For `d36 Jump Float Introduction`: **3 sets of 4 = 12 reps** for the first two sessions; **4 sets of 4 = 16 reps** from session 3 onward if prior sessions were clean (no pain, no >1 h post-session soreness, no next-day pain, no late-session accuracy collapse). 20 s inter-rep, 3 min inter-set, ≤ 2 sessions/week, ≥ 48 h between sessions. This is a change from the prior 3 × 12 = 36 synthesis spec; vendor 3 explicitly rejects "3 sets of 15" as a default introduction volume because (a) the Hurd IHP early-phase template is 4–6 serves/set, (b) an 87-serve jump-float study produced clinically meaningful infraspinatus fatigue in trained D-I players who didn't perceive it, and (c) the 45-rep neighborhood is "hard to defend" for an unsupervised amateur practicing less efficiently than D-I athletes. The 36-rep target is preserved as a **later-wave steady-state drill** (a post-`d36` follow-on that doesn't currently exist in the Tier 1b plan).

2. **A prerequisite gate plus warm-up requirement.** All three vendors agree the gate must be binary pass/fail and self-administrable. Vendor 1's minimum is 2 questions (wall slide + pain/history). Vendor 2 adds smartphone active-flexion and bathroom-scale ER/IR ratio tests. **Vendor 3 adds a mandatory volleyball-specific warm-up before jump-float is even offered in the session flow** — grounded in VolleyVeilig RCT (Baharmast / Gouttebarge: 21 % acute-injury reduction in recreational adult volleyball). **Reconciliation for shipping:** vendor 1's 2-question gate for module-unlock; vendor 3's warm-up requirement as a session-builder prerequisite (ties into the existing Beach Prep Three drill family); vendor 2's ER/IR + active-flexion as optional monthly post-unlock re-checks.

3. **A cue set that is mechanism-ranked, not aesthetic.** All three vendors converge on the triplet: (a) arm pre-loaded in bow-and-arrow / high-elbow position, (b) contact in front of the hitting shoulder from a low / short toss, (c) rigid, flat hand. Vendor 2 adds a fourth cue (~120–130° abduction, avoid hyper-horizontal-adduction; Reeser 2013 BJSM) — track for a later-wave revision. Vendor 3 adds a beach-specific fifth cue: **jump mostly up-and-down on sand, land balanced on two feet** (Tilp 2013 beach-landing evidence; court-dimension rationale).

4. **A progression sequence with an explicit intermediate step.** Vendor 1 documented a 5-step ladder; vendor 2 documented a 6-stage ladder; vendor 3 adds a distinctive intermediate step between "stand-float mastery" and "full-approach jump-float" — the **"slide jump-float"** (Kessel / USAV): quick self-set, minimal toss, arm pre-loaded, simple rhythm. This reduces toss complexity while preserving the overhead timing problem. Worth considering as either (a) a step inside `d36`'s `courtsideInstructions`, or (b) a prequel drill (e.g., a hypothetical `d35 Slide Jump-Float`) in a later Tier 1b wave.

5. **A warning-sign panel split into acute, cumulative, and fatigue-proxy signals.** Acute (stop-now): sharp pinch at arm elevation ~130°; deep posterior pain during cocking; sudden "dead arm"; **repeated toss chasing; unstable landing** (vendor 3 adds). Cumulative (reduce / see physio): posterior ache post-swing; next-day IR loss; mid-session accuracy collapse; night pain; ER-strength loss over weeks; **visible infraspinatus-fossa hollow** (vendor 2's distinctive addition — Lajtai 2009, 30 % prevalence, typically painless). Fatigue-proxy (vendor 3 adds as a distinct category): emerging ball spin after clean contacts; late-set accuracy collapse; overhead-freedom loss hours-after or next-morning. **Vendor 3's Hurd soreness rules as operational copy**: "If sore during warm-up and not normal within the first 15 balls, stop and take two days off. If soreness >1 h post-session or next-day, step down one level rather than progress."

**The divergence that matters most: self-report is not reliable.** The 87-serve fatigue study (cited only by vendor 3) is the evidence that reframes the entire design posture. Players who served 87 jump-floats in a single session showed clinically meaningful infraspinatus fatigue **that they did not accurately perceive**. This means: any ship spec that trusts "I feel fine" as a green light is operating against the evidence. Vendor 3's entire framing — low starting volume, Hurd-derived soreness rules, fatigue-proxy warnings, monthly photo check (which vendor 2 also argues for) — flows from "self-report is insufficient." This is the single most important shift the vendor-3 evidence drives in the synthesis.

**Post-launch feature candidates (vendor-2 + vendor-3).** Not required for day-one `d36` shipping; track as graduated additions:

- **Monthly visual scapula-asymmetry photo check** (vendor 2) — cheap to ship; objective; catches the painless infraspinatus-atrophy pattern pain-based monitoring misses. Ship early.
- **ACWR week-over-week monitoring** (vendor 2) at 1.1 amber / 1.3 red, 7-day acute / 28-day chronic. Requires session history; viable once a multi-session scheduler exists.
- **Annual 4–6 weeks off-overhead** (vendor 2) — calendar nudge.
- **PROMs (KJOC, FAST)** with licensing review (vendor 2).
- **Rep-by-rep error-clustering telemetry** (vendor 3) — do errors cluster after rep 8, 12, or 16? If yes, tighten the cap. This is the post-launch path to replacing extrapolated numbers with cohort evidence.

**Single biggest gap (all three vendors agree).** No peer-reviewed study has quantified jump-float shoulder kinetics as a distinct category, and no prospective dose-response study exists in amateur self-coached beach players. All numerical caps in this note are triangulated from adjacent populations. Vendors 2 and 3 both explicitly reframe this as a product opportunity: an instrumented app can **collect the first real-world cohort evidence** on per-session serve-count thresholds in amateur beach players — closing the gap over time.

**Do-not-ship-without-physio-input blockers (enumerated by vendor 2, reinforced by vendor 3 for items (a) and (h)).** (a) PROM reproduction in-app (KJOC, FAST, QuickDASH) — licensing plus threshold text. (b) GIRD threshold language — literature range is 10–25°; pick one and be transparent; **vendor 3 adds that the recent volleyball prospective cohort did not find a direct GIRD-pain association, raising the question of whether GIRD deserves a hard gate at all in this population.** (c) Age ≥ 50 without overhead-throwing history gate. (d) Infraspinatus-atrophy self-check copy — typically painless, so reassurance framing must be physio-drafted. (e) "Continue to play?" decision logic for any detected cumulative red flag. (f) Suprascapular-neuropathy education copy. (g) All clinical-clearance gate copy for prior surgery, instability, symptomatic SLAP, or rotator-cuff tear. (h) **Prior-injury gate copy** — vendor 3 cites OR 25.3 for shoulder pain from the 2025 cohort; this is the single strongest effect size in the literature and deserves physio-reviewed language.

### Progression sequence

| Step | Content | Risk if skipped |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Stand-float mastery | Consistent toss, rigid contact, weight transfer | Jump-serve with whippy wrist → topspin, compensatory elbow stress |
| 2. Approach + toss, no jump, ball drops | Calibrates toss placement in front of hitting shoulder | Erratic tosses; back-of-head toss → lumbar hyperextension + shoulder hyper-ER |
| 3. Shadow jump, no ball | Grooves the modified beach approach with arms pre-loaded | Full indoor double-arm swing → off-axis loss of balance → anterior-capsule shear |
| 4. Toss + jump + catch at apex | Locks vertical timing; identifies true max contact height | Swinging while descending → net contact or cramped deceleration |
| 5. Full jump-float at 50–75 % | Slow arm speeds for clean-contact tactile feedback | Starting at 100 % → rapid fatigue → scapular-control loss → impingement |

This sequence maps to two concrete product directions: (a) `d36` `courtsideInstructions` can present steps 2–5 inside the drill text rather than bundling all variables at once; (b) a future breakdown into multiple drills (e.g., `d36a` toss-only, `d36b` toss-plus-catch, `d36c` full jump-float) is structurally defensible. Not in Tier 1b scope; tracked for a later wave.

### Warning signs

**Acute (stop-now):**
- Sharp pinch at top of shoulder with arm elevated ≈ 130° (classic subacromial impingement).
- Deep posterior-shoulder pain during cocking phase (internal impingement / SLAP signal).
- Sudden loss of velocity / "dead arm" / heavy or numb sensation (capsular overload, anterior instability, or neurological compression).

**Cumulative (reduce volume or see physio):**
- Post-swing posterior-shoulder ache (posterior cuff fatigue; braking failure).
- Next-day loss of internal rotation (can't tuck shirt in / reach behind back) (acute posterior-capsular stiffness; 72 h overhead rest + active recovery).
- Mid-session accuracy collapse (shanking, dropping elbow, erratic toss) (periscapular stabilizer fatigue; end the session regardless of rep count).

Vendor's ship-spec includes a 24 h post-session push reminder of the cumulative panel, specifically to catch GIRD onset that would otherwise be missed.

### Time-to-competence

- **4–6 weeks** of phased, 1–3 sessions/week practice to reach ~70 % in-court success with zero spin.
- Rushed introduction compounds two risks: (a) motor-engram compensations (e.g., tossing off-line to cover for a mistimed jump) that cap the ceiling, and (b) chronic overloading of shoulder decelerators, which is the pathway to subacute GIRD and tendinopathy over months.

### Sand-specific effects

- CMJ on sand: more upright torso, shorter peak height, lower peak vertical force, prolonged amortization phase.
- SSC utilization: elite ~15.4 % on sand vs ~7.5 % on rigid; amateurs do not access this adaptation and dissipate energy into the sand.
- Design implication: the amateur's instinctive response to lower jump height is to pour more shoulder torque in to clear the net. The bow-and-arrow + low-toss + shortened approach combo explicitly decouples the lower-body jump from the arm swing and prevents that compensation.

### Sand-specific effects

- **Jumping:** Sand reduces VJ height by ~9–14 % (Giatsis 2004 / 2018 / 2022; Bishop 2003; Tilp 2008); SJ peak vGRF ~8 % lower; ankle ROM larger; hip extension range larger. Drop-jump power lower but RFD higher on sand.
- **Key finding for this product:** **upper-limb angular velocities during the spike jump do not differ significantly between sand and indoor** (Tilp 2008). Shoulder cues transfer directly; beach-specific cues are primarily lower-body.
- **Landing:** no force-plate study of volleyball-specific landings on sand vs hard court, but Lian 2005 found patellar tendinopathy dramatically lower in elite beach than elite indoor — consistent with reduced cumulative landing load. Sand training produces less muscle damage and DOMS than equivalent intensity on harder surfaces (Impellizzeri 2008 BJSM).
- **Implication:** jump-float is *particularly appropriate* for sand because it requires only a submaximal jump; the ~10 % sand jump-height penalty has minimal performance cost for this serve. "Jump straight up" cue is driven by court dimensions (8 m vs 9 m) and foot-fault risk, not shoulder load.
- **Gap:** no peer-reviewed study has measured GRF, shoulder torque, or ball velocity during any serve on sand vs hard court. All sand biomechanics come from isolated SJ/CMJ/DJ tasks.

### Sand-specific findings

Vendor 3's sand-specific framing matches vendor 2's more than vendor 1's: vendors 2 and 3 both cite Tilp evidence that arm kinematics transfer from indoor to sand while lower-limb kinematics are substantially modified. Specific numbers vendor 3 emphasises:

- Sand reduces **vertical jump height, vertical GRF, power, and leg stiffness** vs rigid surfaces
- Drop jumps on sand: lower height, lower peak vertical force, lower power, larger knee flexion in downward phase
- Beach-specific landing: professional beach players land on both feet more often than indoor players do; softer surface contributes to lower tendinopathy rates (Lian 2005)
- One-footed landings still appear after faster, more asymmetric actions → jump-float should prefer vertical takeoff + compact approach + two-foot landing

### Time-to-competence

Vendor 3 explicitly declines to set a time-to-competence number, instead defining successful progression by **behavioural criteria**: "consistent in-bounds stand-float serving without pain" → "pain-free reduced-approach jump-float practice" → "two consecutive jump-float sessions with stable accuracy, no next-day pain, and no late-session deterioration in toss control or ball float quality." Vendor 3 notes this definition is "practitioner logic informed by biomechanics and rehab principles, not a formally validated research standard." This is compatible with vendor 1 (4–6 weeks to ~70 % success) and vendor 2 (3–6 months to Stage 5 rally-functional) — just expressed as gates rather than elapsed-time targets.

### Vendor 3's "do not ship" list

Vendor 3 is the only vendor to name a specific volume pattern as "do not ship": **"3 sets of 15 jump-floats" as a default introduction drill**. Rationale: "That volume is not supported by direct evidence, exceeds the spirit of early return-to-hitting reintroduction, and moves too close to a fatigue range that was already problematic in better-trained athletes." This is a direct contestation of the vendor-1-only synthesis spec (3 × 12 = 36) and forces the reconciliation toward vendor 3's conservative position for a first-exposure drill.

## Literature and inference gaps (three-vendor convergence, plus vendor-specific and repo-side additions)

**Gaps all three vendors flag (convergent):**

1. **No peer-reviewed study has quantified jump-float shoulder kinetics as a distinct category.** All biomechanical comparisons pool "jump serve" (typically jump-topspin). Vendor 2: "this is the single most important evidence gap underlying every volume recommendation that follows." Vendor 3: "direct jump-float-versus-stand-float-versus-jump-topspin injury comparisons remain unavailable." Jump-float shoulder load is *inferred* at ~ 65–80 % of spike torque on biomechanical grounds.
2. **No prospective dose-response injury study in adult amateur self-coached beach players** (2–5 years experience, 1–3 sessions/week). All volume anchors are either elite collegiate (Wolfe 2019), post-op rehab (Hurd 2009), or baseball extrapolation (PitchSmart / Fleisig 2011). Every numerical cap in this note is a practitioner-grade extrapolation. Vendor 3 is most explicit: "I did not find a study that says, for example, 'adult amateur beach learners should not exceed 12 jump-float serves per set' or 'more than 30 jump-floats per week increases shoulder injury risk.' That evidence just is not there."
3. **No validated readiness battery for stand-float-to-jump-float progression in this population.** Vendor 3 flags this explicitly; vendors 1 / 2 implicitly. There is no validated screen set that predicts successful progression.
4. **Self-administered screens lose discriminant validity off-clinic.** Vendor 1 flags this for the Arm Care Screen; vendor 2 generalises via Bahr 2016 BJSM ("screening tests flag deficits, do not 'clear' athletes"); vendor 3 reinforces with "this is not a diagnostic test, but it is a reasonable lay screen for a coach-less product."
5. **Self-reported fatigue is unreliable for this serve type (vendor 3 is most explicit).** The 87-serve jump-float fatigue study (vendor 3's primary anchor) showed trained D-I players developed clinically meaningful infraspinatus fatigue **that athletes did not perceive**. This is a direct gap in the self-monitoring premise underlying a coach-less app.

**Gaps vendor 2 flags specifically:**

6. **No force-plate study of any serve on sand vs hard court.** All sand biomechanics come from isolated SJ/CMJ/DJ tasks. Serving-specific sand kinetics are unknown. Tilp 2008 is the strongest anchor for "arm kinematics transfer" but it is spike-specific, not serve-specific.
7. **Pathologic GIRD threshold in volleyball is unsettled (10–25° across papers).** Vendor 3 adds: the 2025 prospective cohort did NOT find a significant direct association between GIRD and pain in volleyball — raising whether the entire GIRD self-test layer deserves the clinical weight overhead-athlete literature generally assigns it.
8. **ACWR "sweet spot" (0.8–1.3) is research-backed but disputed** (Andrade 2020 SR finds mixed evidence). Its volleyball application rests on elite-cohort data; not validated for self-tracked amateurs.
9. **Pieber 2014 vs Lajtai 2012 conflict unresolved** on whether beach infraspinatus atrophy is truly neurogenic (suprascapular-nerve traction) vs biomechanical/disuse. Doesn't change the recommended monthly photo check but does affect how physio-drafted education copy frames the finding.
10. **No validated OASES instrument distinct from KJOC identified.** The "Overhead Athlete Shoulder and Elbow Score" naming appears to be an alternate label for KJOC; confirmation needed with consulting physio.

**Gaps vendor 3 flags specifically:**

11. **No study quantifying whether rushed introduction increases long-term injury risk.** Vendor 3 is the only vendor to flag this. The intuition that shortcutting progression → chronic overload is mechanistically plausible but unvalidated.
12. **Consensus work on return-to-sport and shoulder-injury management acknowledges evidence is thin for many practical decisions.** Not a specific gap, but a meta-point: the whole overhead-athlete management literature is thinner than its clinical use would suggest.

**Repo-side additions (would reshape the defaults if answered):**

13. **Gender-specific or age-stratified amateur volume data absent.** All vendors' caps are population-average. Vendor 2 notes women and female adolescents carry higher non-traumatic overuse shoulder rates (Frisch 2017); vendor 3 notes target users are closer to the adult recreational population in the VolleyVeilig RCT than to elite cohorts — argument for tighter defaults, but no explicit threshold recommended.
14. **Toss-type correlation to injury.** Vendor 1 flags two-handed-vs-one-handed toss as practitioner wisdom with ace-percentage signal but no safety claim. Toss-type × shoulder-load interaction is untested.
15. **Sand-jumping ankle/knee risk.** Vendor 1 is shoulder-centric; vendors 2 and 3 both address the lower-limb side (flatter foot-plant, two-foot landing preference, lower patellar tendinopathy prevalence per Lian 2005) but neither gives concrete ankle/knee thresholds.

**Post-launch opportunity (vendors 2 and 3 converge on framing).** An instrumented app shipping this spec across its user base could **collect the first real-world cohort evidence** on per-session serve-count thresholds in amateur self-coached beach players — closing gaps (2) and (11) over time. High-value telemetry variables vendor 3 specifically names: prior shoulder injury history (gate the analysis), exact jump-float rep count, same-day posterior shoulder pain, next-day overhead pain, visible late-session loss of float quality, and **whether errors begin clustering after rep 8, 12, or 16** (the direct path to validating or tightening the 12-rep first-exposure cap). Vendor 3: "That is where the product can create evidence the literature currently lacks." Vendor 2 similarly proposes telemetry on shank rate, next-day IR loss, infraspinatus-photo monthly deltas, KJOC / FAST scores if/when PROMs are licensed, ACWR series, and session-abandonment reasons.

1. **More conservative wins on safety questions** — especially when one vendor cites direct-evidence anchors the others did not. This principle drives the ship-spec revision from 3 × 12 = 36 to 3 × 4 = 12 for `d36`.
2. **Complementary evidence is additive.** Where one vendor names a product-feature candidate the others did not address (infraspinatus photo check, PROMs, ACWR, Hurd soreness rules, slide jump-float, mandatory warm-up), adopt it as a post-launch candidate — and ship directly if it is cheap, objective, and addresses a signal the others miss.
3. **Contested thresholds route to physio review.** Any number that sits inside a genuinely contested literature range (GIRD threshold, age-50 gate) must be flagged as do-not-ship-without-physio-input before its UI text ships.
4. **Design stance shifts require re-examination of all ship copy.** Vendor 3's "self-report is unreliable" anchor (the 87-serve fatigue study) means any warning-panel or stop-prompt copy that relies purely on "do you feel pain?" needs review. Shipped copy should pair self-report with objective signals (accuracy deterioration, photo check, soreness duration rules).
5. **Progression by behavioural gate is more defensible than by elapsed time.** Ship "advance after 2 clean sessions" (V3) rather than "advance after N weeks."
